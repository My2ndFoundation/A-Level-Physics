# Uniform Electric Field

## Core Idea

A uniform electric field has the same magnitude and direction everywhere; it is produced between two parallel charged plates and relates field strength to plate voltage and separation.

## Meaning

Between two oppositely charged parallel plates, the [[Electric-Field]] is essentially uniform (except near the edges). Its strength is set by the [[Potential-Difference]] across the plates and their separation:

- E = V / d
  - E = electric field strength, V m⁻¹ (equivalently N C⁻¹)
  - V = potential difference between the plates, volts (V)
  - d = plate separation, metres (m)

The field lines (see [[Electric-Field-Line-Diagram]]) are straight, parallel, evenly spaced, and run from the positive plate to the negative plate. Because E is constant, a charge Q between the plates feels a constant force F = EQ, so it accelerates uniformly — directly analogous to projectile motion in a gravitational field.

The two expressions for E, namely F/Q and V/d, are consistent: work done moving charge Q across the plates is W = QV = (force)(distance) = (EQ)d, which rearranges to E = V/d.

## Everyday Intuition

The region between the plates is like a perfectly even ramp: a ball (charge) released anywhere on it rolls "downhill" with the same steady acceleration, no matter where it starts.

## Why It Matters

Uniform fields are used to accelerate and deflect charged particles (oscilloscopes, inkjet printers, mass spectrometers) and describe the field inside a [[Capacitor]]. The constant-force model makes the motion mathematically simple.

## Common Mistakes

- Using E = V/d with non-parallel plates or near the edges where the field is not uniform.
- Mixing units: d must be in metres, giving E in V m⁻¹.
- Assuming the field depends on the charge of a test particle (E depends only on V and d).
