# Equilibrium

## Core Idea

A body is in equilibrium when both the resultant force and the resultant turning effect (moment) acting on it are zero, so it has no acceleration and no angular acceleration.

## Meaning

Equilibrium has two conditions that must hold together:

1. The vector sum of all forces is zero — the [[Resultant-Force]] is zero, so the centre of mass does not accelerate.
2. The sum of moments about any point is zero — clockwise moments balance anticlockwise moments, so the body does not start to rotate.

If only the first condition held, an object could still spin (like a couple acting on a steering wheel). Both conditions are needed for true equilibrium. An object in equilibrium may be stationary (static equilibrium) or moving at constant velocity (dynamic equilibrium); in both cases velocity is unchanging.

For three coplanar non-parallel forces in equilibrium, their lines of action pass through a single point and they form a closed vector triangle. This closed-triangle test is a powerful way to solve problems involving a hanging sign, a ladder, or a strut.

## Everyday Intuition

A book resting on a table: gravity pulls down, the table pushes up by exactly the same amount, nothing moves. A picture hanging straight on a wall: the two cord tensions and the weight balance perfectly.

## Why It Matters

Equilibrium underlies structural engineering, statics problems, and the principle of moments. Recognising that a system is in equilibrium immediately gives equations relating unknown forces.

## Common Mistakes

- Checking only force balance and forgetting moment balance.
- Assuming equilibrium means "at rest" — constant velocity also qualifies.
- Taking moments about an inconvenient point and missing a simplification.
