# Critical Angle

## Core Idea

The critical angle is the angle of incidence inside a denser medium for which light refracts exactly along the boundary; at any larger angle [[Total-Internal-Reflection]] occurs.

## Meaning

At the critical angle $\theta_c$, the angle of refraction is $90^\circ$. Substituting $\theta_2 = 90^\circ$ into [[Snell-Law]] for light passing from a medium of [[Refractive-Index]] $n$ into a vacuum or air gives:

$$\sin\theta_c = \frac{1}{n}$$

More generally, going from index $n_1$ into a less dense medium $n_2$ (with $n_1 > n_2$): $\sin\theta_c = n_2 / n_1$. A higher refractive index gives a smaller critical angle.

## Everyday Intuition

In a glass of water, light from below the surface escapes only if it strikes the surface within a narrow cone; outside this cone the surface looks silvered.

## Why It Matters

The critical angle sets the acceptance condition for light to stay trapped in an optical fibre, which is central to endoscopy in [[Medical-Imaging]] and to communications.

## Experiments or Observations

- Semicircular block method: the incidence angle at which the emerging ray just disappears is the critical angle, giving $n = 1/\sin\theta_c$.

## Common Mistakes

- Inverting the relation as $\sin\theta_c = n$ instead of $1/n$.
- Forgetting the critical angle only exists going from dense to less dense media.
