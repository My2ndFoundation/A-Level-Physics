# Breaking Stress

## Core Idea

Breaking stress is the maximum [[Stress]] a material can withstand before it fractures. It characterises how strong a material is, independent of the size of the test sample.

## Meaning

Stress is force per unit cross-sectional area, $\sigma = F/A$, measured in pascals (Pa, equivalently N m⁻²). As a sample is loaded, the stress rises. The **ultimate tensile stress** (UTS) is the highest stress reached on the [[Stress-Strain-Graph]]; the **breaking stress** is the stress at the point where the sample actually fractures. For brittle materials these are essentially the same; for ductile materials necking causes the breaking point to follow the UTS at a lower apparent stress.

Because stress normalises out the cross-sectional area, breaking stress is a material property: a thick and a thin steel wire of the same alloy break at the same stress, even though the thick wire withstands a larger force. This is why strength is quoted as a stress, not a force.

Strength must be distinguished from related ideas:
- **Stiffness** is resistance to elastic deformation, set by the [[Young-Modulus]] (gradient of the elastic region).
- **Strength** is the stress at failure (breaking stress).
- **Toughness** is the energy absorbed before fracture (area under the curve), linked to [[Elastic-Strain-Energy]] plus plastic work.
- **Brittleness/ductility** describes how much plastic deformation occurs before fracture ([[Elastic-and-Plastic-Behaviour]]).

## Everyday Intuition

A thin fishing line and a thick climbing rope of the same fibre fail at the same breaking stress; the rope simply holds a bigger load because it has more area.

## Why It Matters

Selecting safe load limits for cables, beams and components requires knowing the breaking stress and applying a safety factor so working stress stays well below it.
