# Drag

## Core Idea

Drag is the resistive force exerted by a fluid (a gas or a liquid) on an object moving through it, always acting opposite to the object's velocity relative to the fluid.

## Meaning

As an object moves through a fluid it must push fluid out of the way and overcome internal friction (viscosity). The reaction is a backward force called drag. Its key feature is that it increases with speed: at low speeds drag is roughly proportional to speed, while at the higher speeds typical of falling bodies and vehicles it is approximately proportional to the square of the speed. Drag also depends on the fluid density, the cross-sectional area presented to the flow, and the object's shape (streamlining).

Because drag grows with speed, an accelerating falling object eventually reaches a speed where drag balances weight, the [[Resultant-Force]] becomes zero, and it stops accelerating — this is [[Terminal-Velocity]]. Drag is dissipative: it transfers ordered kinetic energy into thermal energy of the fluid, so mechanical energy is not conserved.

## Everyday Intuition

Putting your hand out of a moving car window — the push you feel grows sharply as the car speeds up. A parachute works by hugely increasing area to create large drag at low speed.

## Why It Matters

Drag governs the design of cars, aircraft, projectiles and parachutes, the existence of terminal velocity, and why real falling motion deviates from ideal [[Free-Fall]].

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming drag is constant rather than speed-dependent.
- Confusing drag with surface friction between solids.
- Forgetting that drag means real falls are not free fall.
