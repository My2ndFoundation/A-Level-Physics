# Astronomical Distances

## Core Idea

Astronomical distances are enormous, so astronomers use special units —
the astronomical unit, light-year and parsec — and indirect measurement
methods such as parallax rather than metres and rulers.

## Meaning

Three standard distance units appear at A-Level.

- **Astronomical unit (AU):** the mean Earth–Sun distance,
  1 AU ≈ 1.50 × 10¹¹ m. Convenient for distances within the Solar System.
- **Light-year (ly):** the distance light travels in one year in vacuum,
  1 ly ≈ 9.46 × 10¹⁵ m. Convenient for interstellar distances.
- **Parsec (pc):** the distance at which 1 AU subtends a parallax angle of
  1 arcsecond, 1 pc ≈ 3.09 × 10¹⁶ m ≈ 3.26 ly.

**Stellar parallax** measures nearby-star distances. As Earth orbits the Sun,
a nearby star appears to shift against the distant background. Half the
angular shift over six months is the parallax angle p. If p is in arcseconds:

$$d\ \text{(parsecs)} = \frac{1}{p\ \text{(arcseconds)}}$$

Smaller parallax means a more distant star, so the method is limited to
relatively nearby stars where p is large enough to measure.

For greater distances, brightness-based methods (using known
[[Luminosity]] standard candles) and recession-based methods using
[[Hubbles-Law]] take over.

## Everyday Intuition

Hold a finger up and close each eye in turn: the finger jumps against the
background. The closer the finger, the bigger the jump — exactly how parallax
gauges stellar distance.

## GCSE Foundation

- [[Wavelength]]

GCSE introduces the light-year qualitatively. A-Level adds the parsec, the
parallax equation and a distance ladder.

## Why It Matters

Distance underpins all of astrophysics: luminosity, the H–R diagram and the
cosmic distance scale all depend on knowing how far away objects are.
