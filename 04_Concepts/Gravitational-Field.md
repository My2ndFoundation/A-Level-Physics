# Gravitational Field

## Core Idea

A gravitational field is a region of space in which a mass experiences a
gravitational force. Every mass produces such a field, and any other mass
placed in it is pulled towards the source mass.

## Meaning

A gravitational field is the modelling tool that lets us describe gravity
without saying that two masses "reach out and touch". Instead, one mass sets
up a field everywhere around it, and a second mass responds to the field at
its own location. The strength of the field at a point is the
[[Gravitational-Field-Strength]] g, defined as the force per unit mass:

g = F / m

with g in N kg⁻¹ (equivalently m s⁻², since it is also the free-fall
acceleration). Gravitational fields are always attractive, so field lines
point inwards towards the mass that produces them.

For a point mass (or outside a uniform sphere) the field is **radial** and
its magnitude follows an inverse-square law derived from
[[Newtons-Law-of-Gravitation]]:

g = G M / r²

where G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻² is the gravitational constant, M is the
source mass and r is the distance from its centre. Close to a large body the
field is approximately **uniform**: near Earth's surface g ≈ 9.81 N kg⁻¹ and
the field lines are essentially parallel.

## Everyday Intuition

A dropped ball always falls towards the ground. The field is the invisible
"slope" of space around Earth that every object rolls down at the same rate
regardless of its mass.

## GCSE Foundation

- [[Weight]]
- [[Mass]]

At GCSE, weight is just W = m g with g a fixed number. A-Level shows g is a
field quantity that varies with distance via an inverse-square law.

## Why It Matters

The field concept unifies falling apples, orbiting satellites, planetary
orbits and tides under one framework. It also parallels the electric field,
helping transfer reasoning between gravitation and electrostatics.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating g as constant everywhere instead of varying as 1/r²
- Confusing field strength (N kg⁻¹) with [[Gravitational-Potential]] (J kg⁻¹)
- Forgetting that fields are always attractive, never repulsive
