# Standing Waves

## Core Idea

A standing (stationary) wave is formed when two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions superpose, producing a pattern of fixed nodes and antinodes that does not transfer net energy along the medium.

## Meaning

Standing waves usually arise when a wave reflects back on itself, for example on a string fixed at both ends or in an air column. By the principle of [[Superposition]] the incident and reflected waves combine to give points that never move (nodes) and points that oscillate with maximum amplitude (antinodes). The distance between adjacent nodes is half a wavelength.

Unlike a progressive wave, a standing wave does not transport energy along the medium; energy stays trapped, swapping between kinetic and potential within each loop. All points between two adjacent nodes oscillate in phase but with different amplitudes; across a node there is a phase reversal.

Standing waves only form at certain frequencies, the resonant frequencies or harmonics, for which a whole number of half-wavelengths fits the boundary conditions. A string fixed at both ends has its fundamental when its length equals half a wavelength; higher harmonics fit more loops. Pipes give similar patterns with antinodes at open ends and nodes at closed ends.

## Everyday Intuition

Plucking a guitar string makes it vibrate in a fixed pattern at a definite pitch. Blowing across a bottle produces a note set by the air-column standing wave.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking a standing wave transfers energy along the medium.
- Confusing the node-to-node distance with a full wavelength (it is half).
- Believing all points oscillate with the same amplitude.
