# Atomic Structure

## Core Idea

An atom is built from protons and neutrons in a central nucleus, surrounded by electrons; the numbers of these particles determine the element, its charge, and its isotope.

## Meaning

The nucleus contains **protons** (charge +e, mass ≈ 1 u) and **neutrons** (charge 0, mass ≈ 1 u), collectively called **nucleons**. **Electrons** (charge −e, mass ≈ 1/1836 u) surround the nucleus. A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons.

Two integers describe a nuclide, written as ᴬ_Z X:

- **Proton number** Z (atomic number): number of protons; fixes the element.
- **Nucleon number** A (mass number): total protons + neutrons. Number of neutrons N = A − Z.

The elementary charge is e = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. The unified atomic mass unit u = 1.66 × 10⁻²⁷ kg.

## Everyday Intuition

Think of the proton number as a postcode that names the element, and the neutron number as the variation between houses on the same street ([[Isotopes]]).

## Why It Matters

Atomic structure underpins chemistry, ionisation, conduction, [[Radioactive-Decay]], and nuclear energy. Changes to Z and A track every nuclear process: alpha decay reduces A by 4 and Z by 2; beta-minus decay leaves A unchanged and raises Z by 1.
