# Vectors and Scalars

## Core Idea

A scalar quantity has magnitude only; a vector quantity has both magnitude and direction. The distinction controls how quantities are added, resolved and used in equations.

## Meaning

A **scalar** is fully specified by a number and a unit: examples are [[Mass]], temperature, time, energy, [[Work]], [[Kinetic-Energy]], speed and electric charge. Scalars combine by ordinary arithmetic.

A **vector** also carries a direction: examples are displacement, [[Velocity]], acceleration, [[Force]], momentum and field strength. Vectors are written in bold ($\mathbf{F}$) or with an arrow, and combine by vector addition, not simple arithmetic — direction must be respected.

Adding vectors: place them tip-to-tail and draw the resultant from the start of the first to the end of the last (the [[Vector-Triangle]] or polygon method), or add components. Two perpendicular vectors of magnitude $a$ and $b$ give a resultant of magnitude $\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}$ at angle $\tan^{-1}(b/a)$ to the first. Conversely, any vector can be split into perpendicular parts — see [[Resolving-Vectors]] — usually $V\cos\theta$ and $V\sin\theta$.

The same physical situation can use both: speed (scalar) is the magnitude of velocity (vector); distance (scalar) is the path length while displacement (vector) is the straight-line change in position. Mixing them up — for example adding forces as if they were numbers — is a frequent error.

## Everyday Intuition

Walking 3 m east then 4 m north leaves you 5 m from the start (vectors), even though you walked 7 m of path (scalar distance).

## Why It Matters

Correctly classifying quantities determines whether you add numbers or use geometry, and underlies force balance, projectile motion, fields and momentum throughout A-Level physics.

## Common Mistakes

- Adding vectors arithmetically instead of with direction
- Confusing speed/distance (scalars) with velocity/displacement (vectors)
- Forgetting that a vector can be negative depending on chosen positive direction
