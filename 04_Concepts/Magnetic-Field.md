# Magnetic Field

## Core Idea

A magnetic field is a region of space in which a moving charge, a current, or a magnetic material experiences a magnetic force. It is a vector field, having both a magnitude and a direction at every point.

## Meaning

A magnetic field is produced by moving charges: by permanent magnets (aligned electron spins and orbital motion), by current-carrying wires, and by changing electric fields. The field is described by the [[Magnetic-Flux-Density]] **B**, measured in tesla (T). The direction of the field at a point is the direction in which the north pole of a small test compass would point — by convention, field lines run from north to south outside a magnet and south to north inside it.

Unlike an [[Electric-Field]], a uniform magnetic field exerts no force on a stationary charge. A force only arises when [[Charge]] moves across the field, or equivalently when a [[Current]] flows through it. This is the basis of the motor effect ([[Force-on-a-Current-Carrying-Conductor]]) and the deflection of moving charges ([[Force-on-a-Moving-Charge]]).

## Everyday Intuition

A fridge magnet snapping onto a steel door, a compass needle swinging to point north, and the click of a relay are all everyday evidence of magnetic fields. The Earth itself behaves like a giant, slightly tilted bar magnet, which is why compasses work.

## GCSE Foundation

- [[Force]]
- [[Current]]

At GCSE the magnetic field is introduced qualitatively as the region around a magnet or current-carrying wire, drawn with field lines and explored with iron filings and plotting compasses. A-Level makes this quantitative through **B** and the force laws.

## Why It Matters

Magnetic fields underpin electric motors, generators, transformers, loudspeakers, particle accelerators, mass spectrometers, and magnetic data storage. The link between changing magnetic fields and induced voltages ([[Electromagnetic-Induction]]) is the foundation of nearly all electrical power generation.

## Frontier Links

- Electromagnetism is unified with electricity in Maxwell's equations and, at high speed, with special relativity — a magnetic field in one frame can appear partly electric in another.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking a magnetic field pushes a stationary charge (it does not — motion is required).
- Confusing field lines (showing direction) with the strength of the field (shown by line spacing).
- Forgetting that field lines run north→south outside but south→north inside a magnet.
