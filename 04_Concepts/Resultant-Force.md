# Resultant Force

## Core Idea

The resultant force is the single force that has the same effect as all the forces acting on a body combined. It is the vector sum of every force on the object.

## Meaning

A real object usually has several forces acting on it at once: weight, normal contact force, tension, friction, drag, applied pushes and pulls. Because force is a vector, these cannot simply be added as numbers — they must be added head-to-tail, taking direction into account. The single arrow that closes the vector polygon, drawn from start to finish, is the resultant.

If the resultant force is zero, the object is in [[Equilibrium]] and continues at constant velocity (or stays at rest), as required by [[Newton-First-Law]]. If the resultant force is non-zero, the object accelerates in the direction of that resultant, with magnitude given by [[Newton-Second-Law]]: the acceleration is proportional to the resultant force and inversely proportional to mass.

To find a resultant in two dimensions we usually resolve each force into perpendicular components, sum the components separately, then recombine using Pythagoras and trigonometry to get magnitude and direction.

## Everyday Intuition

Two people pushing a stalled car the same way add their efforts. Two people pushing from opposite sides partly cancel — only the difference moves the car. A tug-of-war is decided by which side has the larger resultant pull.

## Why It Matters

Almost every mechanics problem begins by finding the resultant force. It is the bridge between the picture of all the forces (a [[Free-Body-Diagram]]) and the motion the object actually undergoes. Without it, Newton's laws cannot be applied numerically.
