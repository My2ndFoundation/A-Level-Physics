# Radioactive Decay

## Core Idea

Radioactive decay is the spontaneous, random disintegration of an unstable atomic nucleus, which emits radiation (alpha, beta or gamma) and changes into a more stable nucleus.

## Meaning

Some nuclei have an unstable combination of protons and neutrons. Such a nucleus will, at an unpredictable moment, transform to become more stable, releasing energy as radiation. The process has two defining statistical properties: it is random (you cannot predict when any individual nucleus will decay) and spontaneous (it is unaffected by temperature, pressure or chemical state).

There are three main emissions. Alpha decay emits a helium nucleus (2 protons, 2 neutrons), strongly ionising but easily stopped. Beta-minus decay converts a neutron into a proton, emitting a fast electron and an antineutrino. Gamma emission is a high-energy photon released as the nucleus loses excess energy, usually after another decay. Nucleon and charge numbers are conserved in every decay, which lets decay equations be balanced.

Although individual decays are unpredictable, a large sample behaves predictably: the activity (decays per second) is proportional to the number of undecayed nuclei, giving exponential decay described by the [[Radioactive-Decay-Law]] and characterised by the [[Half-Life]].

## Everyday Intuition

It is like popcorn in a hot pan — you cannot say which kernel pops next, but you can predict roughly how many pop per second. Carbon dating and smoke detectors rely on this steady statistical behaviour.
