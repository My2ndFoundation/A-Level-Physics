# Energy-Transfer

## Core Idea

Energy transfer is the movement of energy from one store or system to another. Energy itself is never created or destroyed (see [[Conservation-of-Energy]]); what changes in any physical process is *where* the energy is and *which store* it occupies.

## Meaning

A useful model treats energy as held in **stores** (kinetic, gravitational, elastic, thermal, electrical, chemical, nuclear) and moved between them by **transfer pathways**:

- **mechanically** — a force doing [[Work]] on a system,
- **electrically** — charge moving through a potential difference,
- **by heating** — energy flowing because of a temperature difference,
- **by radiation** — energy carried by waves (light, sound, EM).

The amount transferred is measured in joules (J), the same unit as [[Energy-Quantity|Energy]] itself, because a transfer is just energy changing location or form. The *rate* of transfer is [[Power]] (J s⁻¹ = W). Real transfers are rarely complete: some energy is always dissipated, usually to thermal energy in the surroundings, which is why efficiency is less than 100%.

## Everyday Intuition

Pushing a trolley transfers chemical energy in your muscles to the trolley's kinetic store. A phone charger transfers electrical energy to the chemical store of the battery. A hot drink cools because thermal energy is transferred to the room. In every case the total energy is unchanged — it has only moved or spread out.

## Why It Matters

Energy transfer is the backbone of nearly every quantitative argument in mechanics, electricity, thermal and nuclear physics. Tracking transfers between stores is one of the most reliable problem-solving strategies, and it underlies efficiency, conservation arguments, and the analysis of dissipative effects such as [[Friction]].

## Common Mistakes

- Saying energy is "used up" or "lost" — it is transferred or dissipated, never destroyed.
- Confusing an energy *store* with a transfer *pathway* (e.g. calling "heat" a store rather than a heating transfer).
- Mixing the unit of energy (J) with the unit of power (W).
