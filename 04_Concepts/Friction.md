# Friction

## Core Idea

Friction is the contact force that opposes, or tends to oppose, the relative sliding motion between two surfaces in contact.

## Meaning

When two solid surfaces touch, microscopic roughness and adhesion between them resist sliding. Friction always acts along the surface and in the direction that opposes relative motion (or attempted motion).

There are two regimes. Static friction acts when surfaces are not yet sliding; it self-adjusts up to a maximum value to prevent motion. Once sliding begins, kinetic (dynamic) friction acts and is usually slightly smaller and roughly constant. Friction does not depend much on the apparent contact area but does increase with the normal contact force pressing the surfaces together.

Friction converts ordered kinetic energy into thermal energy (heat) and sometimes sound, so mechanical energy is not conserved when friction acts — this connects to [[Conservation-of-Energy]] through dissipative work. It is non-conservative: the energy lost depends on the path taken.

## Everyday Intuition

Rubbing your hands together warms them. A book slides across a table and stops because friction removes its kinetic energy. Walking is only possible because friction between shoe and ground provides grip.

## Why It Matters

Friction appears in nearly every real mechanics problem, in braking, in efficiency calculations, and in [[Terminal-Velocity]] for surface contact. It is both essential (grip, brakes) and wasteful (engine losses).

## Applications

## Frontier Links

- Nanoscale friction and tribology research at the surface-physics edge.

## Common Mistakes

- Drawing friction in the direction of motion instead of opposing it.
- Assuming friction depends on contact area.
- Treating friction as conservative and including it in mechanical-energy conservation.
