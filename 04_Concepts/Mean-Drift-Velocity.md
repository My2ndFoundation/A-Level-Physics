# Mean Drift Velocity

## Core Idea

Mean drift velocity is the slow average velocity of charge carriers superimposed on their rapid random motion when a current flows in a conductor.

## Meaning

Free electrons in a metal move randomly at high speed even with no current. When a [[Potential-Difference]] is applied they acquire a small net drift, typically less than a millimetre per second. The [[Current]] is related to drift velocity by:

$$I = nAve$$

where $n$ is the number density of free carriers ($\text{m}^{-3}$), $A$ the cross-sectional area ($\text{m}^2$), $v$ the mean drift velocity ($\text{m s}^{-1}$), and $e$ the carrier charge (C). For a fixed current, a thinner wire or lower carrier density gives a higher drift velocity.

## Everyday Intuition

Electrons drift like a slow crowd shuffling through a corridor while individually darting about — yet a light comes on instantly because the electric field, not the electrons, propagates near light speed.

## Why It Matters

The equation $I = nAve$ explains why current is the same all around a series circuit, why semiconductors (low $n$) behave differently from metals, and why signals travel fast even though electrons drift slowly. It connects microscopic carrier motion to a measurable current.
