# Total Internal Reflection

## Core Idea

When light travelling in a dense medium meets a boundary with a less dense medium at an angle greater than the critical angle, none of it refracts out — all of it reflects back into the dense medium.

## Meaning

As the angle of incidence inside the dense medium increases, the refracted ray bends further from the normal. At the [[Critical-Angle]] the refracted ray runs along the boundary. Beyond it, [[Snell-Law]] has no real solution for the refracted ray, so 100% of the light is reflected internally and obeys the ordinary law of reflection. Two conditions are needed: light must travel from a higher to a lower refractive index medium, and the angle of incidence must exceed the critical angle.

## Everyday Intuition

Looking up from underwater, beyond a certain viewing angle the surface acts like a mirror and you see the reflection of the pool floor rather than the sky.

## Why It Matters

Total internal reflection guides light along optical fibres with almost no loss, underpinning endoscopes in medicine and high-speed communications. It also explains how prisms can reflect light efficiently in binoculars.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking TIR can occur going from a less dense to a denser medium.
- Confusing partial reflection (always present) with total internal reflection (only beyond $\theta_c$).
