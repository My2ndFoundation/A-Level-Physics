# The Nuclear Atom

## Core Idea

Almost all the mass of an atom and all of its positive charge are concentrated in a tiny dense central nucleus, with electrons occupying the relatively vast space around it.

## Meaning

In the nuclear model the atom is mostly empty space. A central nucleus, around 10⁻¹⁵ m across, carries the protons and neutrons, while the atom as a whole is about 10⁻¹⁰ m across — roughly 10000 to 100000 times larger than the nucleus. Negatively charged electrons are bound to the nucleus by the electrostatic (Coulomb) attraction between them and the protons. This replaced the earlier "plum pudding" picture, in which positive charge was thought to be spread uniformly through the atom.

## Everyday Intuition

Picture a marble (the nucleus) at the centre of a sports stadium (the atom). The structure you "feel" when matter pushes back on you comes almost entirely from electron clouds, not from contact between the dense cores.

## Why It Matters

The nuclear model is the basis for understanding [[Radioactive-Decay]], [[Nuclear-Fission]], [[Nuclear-Fusion]], and atomic spectra. The key experimental evidence is the Geiger–Marsden (Rutherford) alpha-scattering experiment: most alpha particles passed almost straight through a thin gold foil, but a small fraction were deflected through large angles and a very few bounced back. A diffuse positive charge could not produce such strong, rare deflections; only a small, massive, highly charged nucleus could.

## Common Mistakes

- Believing the atom is "solid" rather than mostly empty space
- Confusing nuclear diameter (~10⁻¹⁵ m) with atomic diameter (~10⁻¹⁰ m)
- Thinking electrons orbit like planets at fixed radii (this is a simplification)
