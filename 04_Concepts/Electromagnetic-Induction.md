# Electromagnetic Induction

## Core Idea

Electromagnetic induction is the production of an electromotive force (e.m.f.), and hence a [[Current]] in a closed circuit, whenever the [[Magnetic-Flux-Linkage]] through a conductor changes.

## Meaning

A conductor experiences an induced e.m.f. when the magnetic flux linking it changes. This change can be produced by moving a magnet relative to a coil, moving a conductor through a field so it "cuts" field lines, rotating a coil in a field, or changing the current (and hence field) in a nearby coil. The size of the induced e.m.f. is governed by [[Faradays-Law]], and its direction by [[Lenzs-Law]], which is a consequence of [[Conservation-of-Energy]].

The key requirement is *change*. A coil held stationary in a steady magnetic field has constant flux linkage and zero induced e.m.f. Only the *rate of change* of flux linkage matters, so faster motion, stronger fields, or more turns all increase the induced e.m.f.

## Everyday Intuition

A bicycle dynamo lighting a lamp, the charging pad for a phone, and the hum of a transformer all rely on induction. Moving a fridge magnet quickly past a coil of wire connected to a sensitive meter makes the needle flick — proof that motion creates voltage.

## GCSE Foundation

- [[Current]]
- [[Force]]

GCSE introduces the generator effect qualitatively (moving a wire in a field induces a voltage). A-Level quantifies this with flux linkage and the rate-of-change law.

## Why It Matters

Almost all electrical energy is generated by induction in rotating generators. Induction also explains transformers, induction cooktops, microphones, electric guitar pickups, regenerative braking, and metal detectors.

## Frontier Links

- Induction is one half of Maxwell's coupled electric and magnetic field equations, which together predict electromagnetic waves travelling at the speed of light.

## Common Mistakes

- Believing a steady field induces an e.m.f. (only *changing* flux linkage does).
- Confusing flux with flux linkage (the latter includes the number of turns).
- Forgetting the minus sign / [[Lenzs-Law]] direction, which enforces energy conservation.
