# Superposition

## Core Idea

The principle of superposition states that when two or more waves overlap at a point, the resultant displacement at that point is the vector sum of the individual displacements of each wave.

## Meaning

Waves, unlike solid objects, can pass through one another. While two waves occupy the same region, the medium's displacement at each instant is simply the sum of what each wave would produce alone. After they have passed through each other, each wave continues unchanged — superposition is temporary at any given point but the waves themselves are not altered.

Superposition is the foundation for several major wave phenomena. When two coherent waves overlap continuously it produces [[Interference]] (fixed regions of reinforcement and cancellation). When a wave overlaps with its own reflection it produces [[Standing-Waves]]. When many slits contribute it produces [[Diffraction]] patterns.

The key conditions for a meaningful, observable combined effect (a stable interference pattern) are that the waves have the same frequency, a constant phase relationship (coherence) and similar amplitude. The instantaneous sum can be constructive (in phase, amplitudes add) or destructive (in antiphase, amplitudes subtract).

## Everyday Intuition

Two people talking at once — your ear receives the summed pressure wave. Ripples from two pebbles cross and momentarily build into bigger or flatter patches, then continue on as if nothing happened.

- Thinking waves collide and bounce off each other like particles.
- Believing the waves are permanently changed after overlapping.
- Forgetting displacement is a vector, so antiphase waves subtract.
