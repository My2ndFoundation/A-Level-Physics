# Moment

## Core Idea

The moment of a force is its turning effect about a pivot, equal to the force multiplied by the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force.

## Meaning

Moment $=$ force $\times$ perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action. Its unit is the newton metre (N m). The moment depends not only on how big the force is but on where and in what direction it is applied: a force whose line of action passes through the pivot has zero moment.

Moments have a sense — clockwise or anticlockwise. The principle of moments states that for a body in rotational [[Equilibrium]] the total clockwise moment about any point equals the total anticlockwise moment about that point. Two equal, opposite, parallel forces that do not share a line of action form a couple, whose moment (torque of the couple) equals one force times the perpendicular separation, producing pure rotation with no resultant force.

The perpendicular distance is essential: if a force acts at an angle, you must use the component perpendicular to the line joining pivot and point of application, or equivalently the perpendicular distance to the full line of action.

## Everyday Intuition

A longer spanner makes it easier to undo a tight bolt. Pushing a door near the hinge barely moves it; pushing at the handle swings it easily — same force, different moment.

## Common Mistakes

- Using the straight-line distance instead of the perpendicular distance.
- Forgetting moments can be taken about any point, not just the stated pivot.
- Confusing a couple (pure turning, no resultant force) with a single offset force.
