# Kinetic Energy

## Core Idea

Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses because of its motion. The faster it moves and the more mass it has, the more kinetic energy it carries.

## Meaning

For an object of [[Mass]] $m$ moving at speed $v$, the (translational) kinetic energy is

$$E_k = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}$$

where $E_k$ is in joules (J), $m$ in kilograms (kg) and $v$ in metres per second (m s⁻¹). It is a scalar quantity: it has no direction, only magnitude, and it is never negative because $v^{2} \ge 0$.

The expression follows from the [[Work]] done by a [[Resultant-Force]] accelerating a body from rest: $W = Fd$ and, using the constant-acceleration relations, this rearranges to $\tfrac{1}{2}mv^{2}$. This is the work–energy principle: the net work done on a body equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Because $E_k$ depends on $v^{2}$, doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy. This non-linear dependence explains why stopping distances grow sharply with speed.

## Everyday Intuition

A slow-rolling football is easy to stop with your foot; the same ball kicked hard stings because it carries far more kinetic energy that must be absorbed by your foot doing negative work on it.

## Why It Matters

Kinetic energy underpins energy transfer analysis: braking vehicles, collisions, projectiles, roller coasters, and turbines. It is one half of mechanical energy, exchanged with [[Gravitational-Potential-Energy]] and [[Elastic-Strain-Energy]] under [[Conservation-of-Energy]].

## Common Mistakes

- Using $v$ instead of $v^{2}$, or forgetting the factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$
- Treating $E_k$ as a vector or allowing negative values
- Confusing kinetic energy with momentum ($p = mv$)
