# Elastic and Inelastic Collisions

## Core Idea

In any collision, total momentum is conserved. A collision is *elastic* if total [[Kinetic-Energy]] is also conserved, and *inelastic* if some kinetic energy is transferred to other forms.

## Meaning

For an isolated system (no external resultant force), [[Conservation-of-Momentum]] always holds:

$$\sum m v \; \text{(before)} = \sum m v \; \text{(after)}$$

with momentum a vector, so directions and signs matter. What distinguishes collision types is the energy bookkeeping:

- **Perfectly elastic collision** — total kinetic energy before equals total kinetic energy after. No energy is converted to heat, sound or deformation. True elastic collisions are rare at macroscopic scale (closest examples: low-speed steel balls, gas molecules, subatomic particles).
- **Inelastic collision** — momentum is conserved but total kinetic energy decreases; the "lost" energy becomes internal energy, sound and permanent deformation.
- **Perfectly inelastic collision** — the objects stick together and move with a common velocity afterwards. This converts the maximum kinetic energy consistent with momentum conservation.

To analyse a collision: write the momentum equation (with vector signs) to find unknown velocities, then compare $\sum \tfrac{1}{2}mv^{2}$ before and after to classify the collision and find energy dissipated.

## Everyday Intuition

Newton's-cradle balls rebound almost elastically; two railway trucks coupling together (perfectly inelastic) move off slowly with an obvious "loss" of kinetic energy felt as the clunk.

## Why It Matters

Collision analysis is central to vehicle safety, particle physics scattering, sports impacts, and explosions (the reverse case, where stored energy becomes kinetic energy while momentum stays zero).
