# Wave Reflection

## Core Idea

Reflection is the change in direction of a wave when it meets a boundary, so that the wave returns into the medium it came from, with the angle of reflection equal to the angle of incidence.

## Meaning

When a wave reaches a boundary, part or all of its energy can bounce back. The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, both measured from the normal (the line perpendicular to the surface at the point of contact), and that the incident ray, reflected ray and normal all lie in the same plane.

During reflection the speed, wavelength and frequency of the wave are unchanged because the wave stays in the same medium; only its direction changes. There may also be a phase change: a wave reflecting off a denser or fixed boundary is inverted (a phase change of π), while reflection off a free or less dense boundary is not inverted. This phase behaviour is important for [[Standing-Waves]] and thin-film [[Interference]].

Reflection applies to all waves — light (mirrors), sound (echoes), water ripples and seismic waves — and is described well by the [[Ray-Model-of-Light]] for the geometric aspects.

## Everyday Intuition

Seeing yourself in a mirror, hearing an echo across a valley, and watching ripples bounce off the side of a pond are all reflection.

## Why It Matters

Reflection explains mirrors, echoes, ultrasound and radar, and is the boundary condition that creates standing waves in strings and pipes.

## Frontier Links

- Total internal reflection underpins optical fibre and photonics technology.

## Common Mistakes

- Measuring angles from the surface instead of from the normal.
- Thinking reflection changes the wave's frequency or speed.
- Ignoring the phase inversion at a fixed/dense boundary.
