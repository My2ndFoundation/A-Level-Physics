# Polarisation

## Core Idea

Polarisation is the restriction of the oscillations of a transverse wave to a single plane. It is direct evidence that light is a transverse wave, since longitudinal waves cannot be polarised.

## Meaning

In an unpolarised transverse wave the field oscillates in all directions perpendicular to the direction of travel. A polariser only transmits the component of oscillation parallel to its transmission axis, producing plane-polarised light. Passing light through two polarisers (a polariser and an analyser) and rotating the second one varies the transmitted [[Intensity]]: maximum when the axes are aligned, zero when they are crossed at $90^\circ$.

## Everyday Intuition

Polaroid sunglasses cut glare because reflected light off water or roads is partly polarised horizontally, and the lenses block that plane.

## GCSE Foundation

- [[Wavelength]]

## Why It Matters

Polarisation distinguishes transverse from longitudinal waves (sound, being longitudinal, cannot be polarised). It is used in LCD displays, stress analysis of materials, and reducing reflected glare. Radio and microwave aerials must be aligned to the polarisation of the broadcast signal.

## Common Mistakes

- Claiming sound can be polarised (it cannot — it is longitudinal).
- Forgetting that an ideal single polariser transmits about half of unpolarised intensity.
