# Investigating Electromagnetic Induction

## Aim

To investigate how the induced e.m.f. in a coil depends on the rate of change of [[Magnetic-Flux-Linkage]], and to verify the qualitative predictions of [[Faradays-Law]] and [[Lenzs-Law]].

## Variables

- Independent variable: rate of change of flux linkage — varied via magnet speed, number of coil turns N, or magnet strength.
- Dependent variable: induced e.m.f. (peak voltage from a data logger or oscilloscope).
- Control variables: coil area, coil resistance/circuit, magnet–coil alignment, temperature.

## Apparatus

- Bar magnet (or stack of strong magnets)
- Coil(s) of known turns N and cross-sectional area A
- Data logger with voltage sensor or sensitive oscilloscope (or sensitive centre-zero galvanometer for qualitative work)
- Light gates / motion sensor or ramp to set reproducible magnet speeds
- Clamp stand, leads

## Method

1. Connect the coil to the voltage sensor / data logger.
2. Drop or push the magnet through the coil at a measured, reproducible speed (use a ramp from set heights, or light gates to record speed v).
3. Record the peak induced e.m.f. for each speed.
4. Repeat with different numbers of turns N (same A) and with stronger magnets to vary B.
5. Reverse the magnet's polarity and direction of motion to observe the e.m.f. changing sign — checking [[Lenzs-Law]].
6. Repeat readings and average to reduce random error.

## Measurements

Peak voltage from the data logger trace; magnet speed from light-gate timing or fall height (v = √(2gh)); turns N counted; coil area A measured with a ruler/calipers.

## Data Processing

For a magnet falling through a coil, induced e.m.f. ∝ rate of change of flux linkage. Plot peak e.m.f. against magnet speed v (expected straight line through the origin for fixed N, B, A), and against N (expected straight line through the origin). A linear best-fit through the origin supports [[Faradays-Law]].

## Graph Use

- Axes: peak e.m.f. (y) against magnet speed v (x), or against N.
- A straight line through the origin confirms direct proportionality to rate of change of flux linkage.
- Gradient relates to B, A and N; a non-zero intercept signals systematic error (e.g. timing offset).

## Uncertainty

- Reaction-time / speed-measurement error — reduce with light gates rather than manual timing.
- Variation in magnet trajectory and alignment — use a guide tube.
- Reading the fluctuating peak voltage — average repeats; use a fast-sampling data logger.
- Earth's field and stray fields — keep orientation constant; subtract any baseline.

## Safety / Practical Limits

Strong neodymium magnets can pinch fingers and damage equipment — handle carefully and keep away from electronics. Catch dropped magnets to avoid damage.

## Common Mistakes

- Concluding the e.m.f. depends on flux rather than its *rate of change*.
- Inconsistent magnet alignment between runs, adding scatter.
- Ignoring the sign reversal that demonstrates [[Lenzs-Law]].
