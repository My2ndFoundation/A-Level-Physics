# Car Safety

## Problem Context

Car safety features — seatbelts, airbags, crumple zones — are an exam favourite because they let physics explain something the learner already understands intuitively. In a crash a passenger's momentum must be reduced to zero. Safety design controls *how* that change of momentum happens, specifically by extending the time over which it occurs.

## Mathematical Tools

- Impulse–momentum relation: force equals rate of change of momentum
- Energy transfer calculations for kinetic energy dissipated in a collision
- Average force from `F = Δp / Δt`

## Typical Questions

- Calculate the average force on a passenger with and without an airbag.
- Explain, using impulse, why a crumple zone reduces injury.
- Use conservation of momentum to find the common velocity in a two-car collision.
- Compare energy absorbed by a crumple zone with the kinetic energy before impact.

## Method Outline

1. Identify the change in momentum the passenger or vehicle must undergo.
2. Identify the collision time, noting how safety features extend it.
3. Apply the impulse–momentum relation to find the average force.
4. For multi-body collisions, apply conservation of momentum first.
5. Compare forces or energies with and without the safety feature.

## Assumptions

- The collision is analysed over a short, well-defined time interval.
- External horizontal forces (friction, braking) are small compared with impact forces.
- The passenger moves with the vehicle until restrained.
