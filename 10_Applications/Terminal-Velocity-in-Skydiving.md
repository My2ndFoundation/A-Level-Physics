# Terminal Velocity in Skydiving

## Problem Context

A skydiver leaving an aircraft does not accelerate indefinitely. As speed rises, air resistance grows until it balances weight, and the skydiver falls at a steady maximum speed. Opening a parachute increases drag dramatically, causing rapid deceleration to a new, lower terminal velocity for a safe landing. This is the standard A-Level context for motion with a velocity-dependent resistive force.

## Mathematical Tools

- Force balance at terminal velocity: weight equals drag
- Newton's second law during the transient phase, where acceleration changes with speed
- Interpreting velocity–time graph gradients and asymptotes

## Typical Questions

- Sketch and explain the velocity–time graph for a complete jump including parachute opening.
- Explain why acceleration decreases as speed increases during free fall.
- State the condition for terminal velocity in terms of forces.
- Explain why the terminal velocity after the parachute opens is lower.

## Method Outline

1. Draw a free-body diagram for the skydiver at the chosen instant.
2. Identify weight (constant) and drag (increasing with speed).
3. Apply Newton's second law: resultant force gives acceleration.
4. Set resultant force to zero to find the terminal velocity condition.
5. Track changes when drag conditions change (parachute deployment).

## Assumptions

- Drag increases monotonically with speed (often modelled as proportional to speed or speed squared).
- Weight is constant over the fall.
- The skydiver's orientation and cross-sectional area are treated as fixed in each phase.
