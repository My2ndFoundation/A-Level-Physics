# Optical Fibres

## Problem Context

Optical fibres carry telephone, internet and television data as pulses of light over long distances with very low loss. They also guide light in medical endoscopes. The physics relies on light staying trapped inside a thin glass core by total internal reflection, which depends directly on refraction at the core–cladding boundary.

## Physical Ideas

- [[Wave-Refraction|Refraction of Waves]]

## Mathematical Tools

- Snell's law relating angles and refractive indices at a boundary
- Critical angle condition for total internal reflection
- Refractive index defined from the speed of light in the medium

## Typical Questions

- Calculate the critical angle for a given core and cladding.
- Explain why the cladding must have a lower refractive index than the core.
- Explain causes of pulse broadening and signal loss along a fibre.
- Compare optical fibre transmission with copper cable.

## Method Outline

1. Identify the refractive indices of the core and cladding.
2. Apply the critical angle condition at the core–cladding boundary.
3. Confirm that rays strike the boundary above the critical angle so they totally internally reflect.
4. Relate ray path length differences to pulse broadening.
5. Discuss attenuation and the need for repeaters over long distances.

## Assumptions

- The fibre is treated as having a uniform core refractive index (step-index model).
- The boundary is smooth and clean.
- Light is monochromatic unless dispersion is being discussed.

## Links to Other Subjects

- Computer Science: optical fibres form the physical layer of high-bandwidth digital networks.
