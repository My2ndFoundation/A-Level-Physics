# Particle Accelerators

## Problem Context

Particle accelerators speed charged particles to very high energies using electric fields and steer them with magnetic fields. They probe the structure of matter, produce medical isotopes, and drive cancer therapy beams. A-Level questions use accelerators to apply work done by electric fields, circular motion in magnetic fields, and energy–momentum reasoning.

## Mathematical Tools

- Work done by an accelerating potential difference giving kinetic energy
- Magnetic force providing centripetal force for circular paths
- Relating radius of curvature to momentum and magnetic flux density
- Energy in electronvolts and unit conversion

## Typical Questions

- Calculate the speed gained by a charge accelerated through a known potential difference.
- Find the radius of the circular path of a charged particle in a magnetic field.
- Explain why a magnetic field changes direction but not speed.
- Explain why higher energies are needed to resolve smaller structures.

## Method Outline

1. Use the accelerating potential difference to find kinetic energy gained.
2. Convert to speed (using a non-relativistic model at A-Level unless told otherwise).
3. Equate the magnetic force to the centripetal force for circular motion.
4. Solve for the unknown radius, field strength, or momentum.
5. Interpret the result in terms of detector design or beam control.

## Assumptions

- Particle motion is treated non-relativistically at A-Level unless stated.
- Fields are uniform in the region considered.
- Energy losses (radiation, collisions) are neglected in basic calculations.
