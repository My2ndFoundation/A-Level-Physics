# Stars and Cosmology

## Problem Context

Stars are natural laboratories: their light tells us their temperature, composition, distance and motion. On the largest scale, the spectra of distant galaxies reveal that the Universe is expanding. A-Level astrophysics applies energy, radiation laws and the Doppler effect to objects far beyond any laboratory.

- [[Conservation-of-Energy]]
- [[Refraction-of-Waves]]

## Mathematical Tools

- Inverse-square law for radiated power and observed intensity
- Relationship between a star's peak emission wavelength and its temperature
- Doppler shift relating wavelength change to recession speed
- Use of standard candles to estimate distance

## Typical Questions

- Estimate a star's surface temperature from its peak wavelength.
- Use observed brightness and known luminosity to estimate distance.
- Calculate a galaxy's recession speed from its red shift.
- Explain how red shift supports an expanding-Universe model.

## Method Outline

1. Identify the observed quantity (brightness, spectrum, wavelength shift).
2. Choose the relevant law (radiation law, inverse-square law, Doppler relation).
3. Substitute carefully, keeping astronomical units consistent.
4. Interpret the result physically (temperature, distance, recession speed).
5. Connect individual measurements to the larger cosmological picture.

## Assumptions

- Stars are treated as ideal black-body radiators for first estimates.
- Space between source and observer is effectively transparent.
- Red shift is interpreted as cosmological expansion for distant galaxies.
