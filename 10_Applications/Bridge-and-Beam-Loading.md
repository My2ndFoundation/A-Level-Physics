# Bridge and Beam Loading

## Problem Context

Bridges, beams and supporting cables must carry loads without breaking or deflecting too far. Engineers choose materials and cross-sections so that the stress stays well below the breaking stress and the structure stays within its elastic region. A-Level questions use this context to apply stress, strain, the Young modulus, and moments to real structures.

## Mathematical Tools

- Stress as force per unit cross-sectional area
- Strain as fractional change in length
- Young modulus as the gradient of the linear stress–strain region
- Moments to find support reactions on a loaded beam

## Typical Questions

- Calculate the extension of a steel cable supporting a known load.
- Find the stress in a beam and compare it with the material's breaking stress.
- Use moments to find the reaction forces at two bridge supports.
- Explain why a thicker or stiffer material reduces deflection.

## Method Outline

1. Draw a free-body diagram of the beam or cable showing loads and supports.
2. Apply the principle of moments to find unknown support forces.
3. Calculate stress from force and cross-sectional area.
4. Use the Young modulus to find strain and hence extension.
5. Check the working stress lies safely within the elastic region.

## Assumptions

- The material behaves elastically and obeys Hooke's law in the working range.
- Cross-sectional area is uniform and the load is applied as stated.
- The beam is light unless its weight is explicitly given.
