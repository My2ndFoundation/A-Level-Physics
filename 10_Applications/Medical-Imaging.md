# Medical Imaging

## Problem Context

Medical imaging lets clinicians see inside the body without surgery. X-rays reveal dense tissue and bone, ultrasound uses reflected sound at tissue boundaries, and gamma-camera techniques follow radioactive tracers. Each method applies a different branch of physics, and A-Level questions ask learners to explain the physical principle and the trade-offs of each technique.

## Mathematical Tools

- Exponential attenuation of X-ray intensity through tissue
- Reflection coefficient at acoustic impedance boundaries for ultrasound
- Inverse-square spreading of radiation intensity
- Half-life calculations for radioactive tracers

## Typical Questions

- Explain how contrast between bone and soft tissue arises in an X-ray image.
- Explain why a coupling gel is used in ultrasound scanning.
- Calculate the activity of a tracer remaining after a given time.
- Compare resolution, penetration and risk for different imaging methods.

## Method Outline

1. Identify the imaging modality and the physical quantity it detects.
2. State how the signal interacts with tissue (absorption, reflection, emission).
3. Apply the relevant law (attenuation, impedance matching, decay).
4. Relate the measured signal to the image contrast or location.
5. Evaluate resolution, penetration depth, and patient risk.

## Assumptions

- Tissue properties are taken as uniform within each region for first calculations.
- Detectors respond linearly over the working range.
- Tracer behaviour follows a single exponential decay.
