# Semiconductor Physics Map

## What This Field Studies

Semiconductor physics studies materials whose ability to conduct electricity
sits between conductors and insulators and can be controlled — by temperature,
light, or by adding tiny amounts of other elements ("doping"). It explains how
silicon devices like diodes, transistors, solar cells, and microchips work at
the level of electrons in solids.

## Why It Matters

Essentially all modern electronics — computers, phones, sensors, LEDs, solar
panels — rests on semiconductor devices. It is the bridge between
[[Quantum-Mechanics-Map|quantum behaviour of electrons]] and everyday
technology, and it makes [[Quantum-Computing-Map|some quantum hardware]]
possible too.

## Key Ideas at the Edge

Names to recognise — not to master:

- **Energy bands** — allowed electron energies group into bands in a solid.
- **Band gap** — the energy step a semiconductor's electrons must jump.
- **Doping (n-type / p-type)** — adding impurities to control conduction.
- **The p–n junction** — the basis of diodes and solar cells.
- **The transistor** — a voltage-controlled switch/amplifier; the chip building block.
- **Photovoltaic effect** — light generating a voltage in a junction.

## What Not to Worry About Yet

Out of scope at A-Level and deliberately omitted here:

- Quantum band-structure mathematics and Bloch functions.
- Fermi–Dirac statistics and carrier-density derivations.
- Semiconductor device fabrication and process engineering.
- Treating advanced device models as needed for A-Level.

Recognising the vocabulary and the device ideas is enough at this stage.

## Useful Analogies

The band gap is like a step electrons must climb to start conducting:
insulators have a huge step, conductors none, semiconductors a small one that
heat or light can help electrons over.
