# Particle Physics Map

## What This Field Studies

Particle physics asks what everything is *ultimately* made of and how those
pieces interact. It studies the smallest known constituents of matter
(quarks and leptons) and the force-carrying particles that bind or transform
them, by smashing particles together at high energy and watching what comes
out.

## Why It Matters

It is our deepest tested description of matter and three of the four
fundamental forces. It explains where mass comes from (the Higgs mechanism),
why matter survived over antimatter, and connects to the first instants of
the universe. Its tools also drive real technology — accelerators and
detectors underpin [[Medical-Physics-MOC|medical imaging]] and cancer
therapy.

## Key Ideas at the Edge

Names to recognise — not to master:

- **The Standard Model** — the catalogue of quarks, leptons and force carriers.
- **Quarks and leptons** — matter comes in three "generations"; everyday matter uses only the first.
- **Force carriers** — photon (electromagnetism), gluons (strong), W and Z (weak).
- **The Higgs boson** — the excitation of the field that gives particles mass (confirmed at CERN, 2012).
- **Antimatter** — every particle has an antiparticle; the matter–antimatter asymmetry is unexplained.
- **Accelerators and detectors** — how particles are created and identified (e.g. the LHC).

## What Not to Worry About Yet

Out of scope at A-Level and deliberately omitted here:

- Quantum field theory, Lagrangians, gauge symmetry.
- Feynman-diagram calculation rules and cross-section maths.
- Group theory (SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1)), renormalisation.
- Speculative physics (supersymmetry, string theory) as if settled.

Recognising the vocabulary is enough at this stage.

## Useful Analogies

The Standard Model is like a periodic table for the truly fundamental
particles — a small chart of building blocks plus the "glue" between them.
