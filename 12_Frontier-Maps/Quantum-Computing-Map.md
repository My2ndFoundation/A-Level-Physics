# Quantum Computing Map

## What This Field Studies

Quantum computing studies how to process information using quantum systems
instead of ordinary bits. Where a classical bit is 0 or 1, a quantum bit
("qubit") can be in a combination of both at once, and qubits can be linked
("entangled"). The field explores which problems this could solve far faster,
and how to build hardware that holds quantum states long enough to be useful.

## Why It Matters

It could speed up specific tasks — simulating molecules and materials, certain
search and optimisation problems, and breaking some current encryption —
beyond what classical computers can do. It is a direct technological
application of [[Quantum-Mechanics-Map|quantum mechanics]] and is driving large
research investment worldwide.

## A-Level Entry Points

Which A-Level (H556) ideas connect here:

- [[Photoelectric-Effect]] — photons as discrete quantum carriers (photonic qubits)
- [[Energy-Levels]] — discrete atomic states used to store a qubit
- The superposition idea (introduced via [[Wave-Particle-Duality]])
- [[Wave-Particle-Duality]] — quantum behaviour with no classical analogue

## Key Ideas at the Edge

Names to recognise — not to master:

- **Qubit** — a quantum bit that can be in a superposition of 0 and 1.
- **Superposition** — being in a blend of states until measured.
- **Entanglement** — qubits correlated so strongly they act as one.
- **Quantum gates** — operations that transform qubit states.
- **Decoherence** — loss of quantum behaviour through disturbance; the main obstacle.
- **Quantum algorithms** — e.g. Shor's and Grover's, names only.
- **Error correction** — using many physical qubits to make one reliable one.

## What Not to Worry About Yet

Out of scope at A-Level and deliberately omitted here:

- Qubit state vectors, Bloch-sphere and gate matrix mathematics.
- Quantum circuit design and algorithm derivations.
- Hardware engineering (superconducting, trapped-ion, photonic detail).
- Hype: treating large-scale fault-tolerant machines as already here.

Recognising the vocabulary and the core promise is enough at this stage.

## Useful Analogies

A classical bit is a coin showing heads or tails; a qubit is like a spinning
coin that is "both" until it lands (is measured). The analogy is loose — it
only hints at superposition.
