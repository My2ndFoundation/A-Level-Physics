# Chaos and Complexity Map

## What This Field Studies

Chaos and complexity study systems that follow fixed rules yet behave in ways
that are very hard to predict. Chaos theory looks at how tiny differences in
starting conditions can grow into completely different outcomes ("sensitive
dependence"). Complexity science looks at how simple parts interacting in
large numbers produce rich, organised behaviour that no single part contains.

## Why It Matters

It explains why weather, fluid turbulence, populations, and many physical
systems are deterministic but practically unpredictable in the long term. It
reframes what it means to "solve" a system: knowing the equations is not the
same as being able to forecast far ahead. It connects directly to how A-Level
physics uses [[Newton-Second-Law|deterministic models]] and to the limits of
modelling.

## A-Level Entry Points

Which A-Level (H556) ideas connect here:

- [[Newton-Second-Law]] — deterministic equations of motion that can still be chaotic
- Modelling assumptions and their limits in mechanics
- Sensitivity to initial conditions (e.g. projectile and oscillator models)
- Iterative/numerical methods used to model motion (Modelling, H556 Module 3)

## Key Ideas at the Edge

Names to recognise — not to master:

- **Deterministic chaos** — exact rules, yet unpredictable long-term behaviour.
- **Sensitive dependence ("butterfly effect")** — tiny changes amplify hugely.
- **Nonlinearity** — outputs not proportional to inputs; the usual source of chaos.
- **Strange attractors** — the structured patterns chaotic systems settle onto.
- **Fractals** — self-similar structure repeating across scales.
- **Emergence** — collective order arising from many simple interactions.
- **The double pendulum** — a classic, easily demonstrated chaotic system.

## What Not to Worry About Yet

Out of scope at A-Level and deliberately omitted here:

- Lyapunov exponents and bifurcation-diagram mathematics.
- Phase-space and dynamical-systems formalism.
- Fractal dimension calculations.
- Treating "complexity" as a single settled theory.

Recognising the vocabulary and the core idea — deterministic yet
unpredictable — is enough at this stage.

## Useful Analogies

Predicting a chaotic system is like balancing a pencil on its tip: the rules
are simple and exact, but the tiniest nudge sends it a completely different
way. Long-range weather forecasting hits the same wall.
