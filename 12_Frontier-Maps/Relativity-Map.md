# Relativity Map

## What This Field Studies

Relativity studies how space, time, motion, and gravity look when speeds
approach the speed of light or when gravity is strong. Special relativity
(1905) deals with constant high-speed motion and the constancy of light speed.
General relativity (1915) extends this to gravity, describing it not as a
force but as the bending of spacetime by mass and energy.

## Why It Matters

It corrects [[Newton-Second-Law|Newtonian mechanics]] at extreme speeds and
strong gravity. It gives the famous mass–energy relationship behind
[[Nuclear-Fission]] and [[Nuclear-Fusion]], explains why GPS satellites need
clock corrections, and underpins our understanding of black holes,
gravitational waves, and the [[Cosmology-Map|expanding universe]].

## A-Level Entry Points

Which A-Level (H556) ideas connect here:

- [[Newton-Second-Law]] — the everyday limit that relativity refines at high speed
- [[Newtons-Law-of-Gravitation]] — Newtonian gravity that general relativity replaces in strong fields
- Mass–energy equivalence and nuclear binding energy — H556 Module 6.4 ([[Nuclear-Fission]], [[Nuclear-Fusion]])
- Frames of reference and relative velocity in mechanics

## Key Ideas at the Edge

Names to recognise — not to master:

- **Constant speed of light** — light's speed is the same for all observers.
- **Time dilation** — moving clocks run slow relative to a stationary observer.
- **Length contraction** — fast-moving objects measure shorter along motion.
- **Mass–energy equivalence** — energy and mass are two sides of the same thing.
- **Spacetime** — space and time treated as one four-dimensional fabric.
- **Curved spacetime** — mass and energy bend spacetime; this *is* gravity.
- **Gravitational waves & black holes** — predicted, then observed phenomena.

## What Not to Worry About Yet

Out of scope at A-Level and deliberately omitted here:

- Lorentz transformation algebra and four-vectors.
- The Einstein field equations and spacetime-curvature mathematics.
- Tensor calculus, metrics, geodesics.
- Treating speculative ideas (wormholes, time travel) as established.

Recognising the core surprises and the vocabulary is enough at this stage.

## Useful Analogies

Spacetime curvature is often pictured as a heavy ball on a stretched sheet:
nearby objects roll toward it. The analogy is rough — it hints at the idea
without being the real mathematics.
