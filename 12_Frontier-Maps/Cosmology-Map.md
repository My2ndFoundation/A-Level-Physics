# Cosmology Map

## What This Field Studies

Cosmology studies the universe as a whole — how it began, how it has changed,
what it is made of, and how it might end. It treats the entire observable
universe as one physical system and asks large-scale questions: why galaxies
are flying apart, what 95% of the universe (that we cannot see) is, and what
the first moments after the Big Bang were like.

## Why It Matters

It is physics applied at the largest possible scale, and it is where several
A-Level threads (gravity, light, spectra, the nuclear atom) combine. It also
exposes the biggest open problems in modern physics — dark matter and dark
energy — reminding us how much remains unknown.

## Key Ideas at the Edge

Names to recognise — not to master:

- **The Big Bang** — the universe began hot and dense ~13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding.
- **Hubble expansion & redshift** — distant galaxies recede; their light is stretched (redshifted).
- **Cosmic microwave background** — faint leftover radiation, a "baby photo" of the universe.
- **Dark matter** — unseen mass that holds galaxies together (more than ordinary matter).
- **Dark energy** — whatever is accelerating the expansion (most of the universe's energy budget).
- **Black holes & neutron stars** — extreme endpoints of stellar evolution.
- **Exoplanets** — planets around other stars, found by transits and wobble.

## What Not to Worry About Yet

Out of scope at A-Level and deliberately omitted here:

- General relativity field equations, spacetime curvature maths.
- Friedmann/FLRW equations and metric cosmology.
- Inflation-model details and structure-formation simulations.
- Treating dark matter/energy candidates as settled — they are open problems.

Recognising the vocabulary and the big picture is enough at this stage.

## Useful Analogies

The expanding universe is like dots on an inflating balloon — every dot sees
every other dot moving away, with no single centre.
