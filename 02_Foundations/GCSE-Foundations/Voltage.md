# Voltage

## Core Idea

Voltage, also called potential difference, is the energy given to (or taken from) each unit of charge as it moves between two points in a circuit. It is the "push" that drives a current.

## Prior Knowledge Level

GCSE

## Meaning

At GCSE, voltage is introduced as the energy transferred per unit charge. It is measured in volts (V) using a voltmeter connected in parallel across the component, so the meter measures the difference in electrical "level" between its two ends.

A cell or power supply provides a potential difference that does work on charges, giving them energy. As those charges pass through components such as resistors, lamps or motors, they transfer that energy to the component (as heat, light, movement, and so on). The voltage *across* a component is the energy transferred to that component per unit charge.

Key GCSE circuit rules:

- In a series circuit, the supply voltage is shared between the components.
- In a parallel circuit, each branch has the full supply voltage across it.

Voltage and current are often confused. Current is the *flow* of charge; voltage is the *energy per unit charge* that causes and accompanies that flow.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

Potential difference is fundamental to A-Level electricity: it appears in [[Ohms-Law]], electrical power and energy, electromotive force and internal resistance, potential dividers, capacitor charging, and electric potential in fields. The GCSE idea of "energy per unit charge" carries straight through.

## Everyday Intuition

If current is like the rate of water flow, voltage is like the pressure difference (or the height of a waterfall) that drives the flow. A taller drop gives each litre of water more energy, just as a higher voltage gives each unit of charge more energy.

## Common Misconceptions

- Confusing voltage with current (voltage does not "flow")
- Thinking voltage is used up like a fuel rather than being energy transferred per charge
- Believing a battery supplies a fixed current regardless of the circuit

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level, potential difference is defined precisely as the work done (energy transferred) per unit charge, $V = W/Q$, and is distinguished carefully from electromotive force. It is combined with [[Electric-Current]] and [[Resistance]] through [[Ohms-Law]], used in potential dividers, and generalised into electric potential and potential energy in fields. See the A-Level [[Potential-Difference]] physical-quantity page for the formal treatment.
