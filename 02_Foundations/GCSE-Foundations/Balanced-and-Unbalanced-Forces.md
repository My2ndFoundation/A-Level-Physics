# Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

## Core Idea

When the forces on an object cancel out, the forces are balanced and the
object's motion does not change. When they do not cancel, the forces are
unbalanced and the object speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.

## Prior Knowledge Level

- GCSE

## Meaning

Every force has a size and a direction. To decide what happens to an object,
add up all the forces, taking direction into account, to find the overall (net)
force. If this overall force is zero the forces are *balanced*; if it is not
zero the forces are *unbalanced* and there is a [[Resultant-Force]].

Balanced forces: object stays still, or keeps moving at a steady speed in a
straight line. Unbalanced forces: object accelerates in the direction of the
net force.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

This idea becomes [[Newton-First-Law]] and [[Newton-Second-Law]]. It underpins
[[Equilibrium]], free-body diagrams, and almost every mechanics problem.

## Everyday Intuition

In a tug-of-war where neither side moves, the pulls are balanced. When one
team pulls harder, the forces become unbalanced and the rope moves their way.

## Common Misconceptions

- Thinking a moving object must have a forward force on it (a steadily moving
  object can have balanced forces).
- Forgetting that [[Friction]] is itself a force to include when balancing.

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level the words "balanced/unbalanced" are replaced by the precise
statement that the vector sum of forces is zero or non-zero, and the net force
is linked to acceleration through F = ma.
