# Atoms

## Core Idea

An atom is the basic building block of ordinary matter. It has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at its centre, surrounded by negatively charged electrons.

## Prior Knowledge Level

GCSE

## Meaning

At GCSE, the atom is described using a nuclear model. Almost all of the atom's mass is concentrated in a central nucleus made of protons (positive charge) and neutrons (no charge). Electrons (negative charge) occupy the space around the nucleus in energy levels or shells. The atom is mostly empty space, and overall a neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons so the charges balance.

Key terms introduced are the atomic number (number of protons, which defines the element) and the mass number (protons plus neutrons). Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

GCSE also introduces how the model developed historically: from the early "plum pudding" idea to the nuclear model supported by the alpha-particle scattering experiment, which showed the positive charge and mass are concentrated in a tiny nucleus.

Atoms can gain or lose electrons to become charged ions. Some nuclei are unstable and undergo radioactive decay, emitting alpha, beta or gamma radiation, which leads into the basics of nuclear physics.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

The atomic model underpins A-Level work on the nucleus, radioactive decay and half-life, nuclear energy and mass–energy equivalence, particle physics and the structure of matter, energy levels and line spectra, and the photoelectric effect. The GCSE nuclear model is the launchpad for all of this.

## Everyday Intuition

Everything around you — air, water, your own body — is made of atoms far too small to see, about a tenth of a billionth of a metre across. The reason solids feel solid even though atoms are mostly empty space is the electrical forces between them, not the atoms physically filling all the space.

## Common Misconceptions

- Picturing electrons as orbiting like planets at fixed visible distances
- Thinking the atom is a solid sphere rather than mostly empty space
- Confusing atomic number and mass number

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level, the model is refined into discrete energy levels that explain line spectra, the nucleus is studied quantitatively through nuclear equations, binding energy and E = mc², radioactive decay is treated with the exponential decay law and half-life, and the substructure of protons and neutrons (quarks) is introduced in particle physics. See the A-Level nuclear and particle physics pages for the formal treatment.
