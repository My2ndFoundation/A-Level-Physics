# Electric Current

## Core Idea

Electric current is the flow of electric charge around a circuit. The bigger the current, the more charge passes a point each second.

## Prior Knowledge Level

GCSE

## Meaning

At GCSE, current is described as the rate of flow of charge. In metal wires the charge is carried by free electrons that drift through the metal when a power source pushes them. Current is measured in amperes (amps, A) using an ammeter connected in series, so that the same current that flows through the component flows through the meter.

Current can be calculated as charge divided by time: more charge flowing, or the same charge flowing in less time, means a larger current.

Two important circuit facts are introduced at GCSE:

- In a series circuit, the current is the same everywhere — charge is not used up as it goes round.
- In a parallel circuit, the current splits between the branches and recombines, so the total current from the source equals the sum of the branch currents.

A common convention is also introduced: *conventional current* is taken to flow from the positive terminal to the negative terminal of a supply, which is opposite to the actual direction electrons drift.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

Current is one of the foundation quantities of A-Level electricity. It underlies [[Ohms-Law]], resistance, power in circuits, Kirchhoff's current law, charge and capacitance, and the microscopic model $I = nAvq$. A clear GCSE picture of "current as flow of charge that is conserved at junctions" is the prerequisite.

## Everyday Intuition

Current is like the rate at which water flows past a point in a pipe. A wider, less restricted path lets more flow per second. Switching on more devices in parallel draws more total current from the supply, just as opening more taps draws more water from the mains.

## Common Misconceptions

- Thinking current is "used up" by components as it goes round a series circuit
- Believing electrons travel at near light speed around the circuit (the drift is actually slow; the effect is fast)
- Confusing current with voltage

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level, current is defined precisely as the rate of flow of charge, $I = \Delta Q / \Delta t$, and is linked to the microscopic picture through $I = nAvq$, where n is charge-carrier density, A cross-sectional area, v drift velocity and q the carrier charge. It is combined with [[Voltage]] and [[Resistance]] via [[Ohms-Law]] and Kirchhoff's laws. See the A-Level [[Current]] physical-quantity page for the formal treatment.
