# Force

## Core Idea

A force is a push or a pull that one object exerts on another. A force can change an object's speed, its direction of motion, or its shape.

## Prior Knowledge Level

GCSE

## Meaning

At GCSE level, a force is understood as an interaction between two objects. Forces can be contact forces (friction, normal contact force, air resistance, tension) or non-contact forces that act at a distance (gravity, magnetism, electrostatic force).

Force is measured in newtons (N). One newton is roughly the size of the gravitational pull on a small apple. A force has both a size and a direction, so it is drawn as an arrow: the length shows how big it is and the way it points shows its direction.

When several forces act on an object, what matters is the overall, combined effect — the resultant force. If forces are balanced (resultant force zero), a stationary object stays still and a moving object keeps moving at a steady speed in a straight line. If forces are unbalanced (resultant force not zero), the object speeds up, slows down, or changes direction. The bigger the resultant force, and the smaller the mass, the greater the change in motion.

Weight is a particular force: the pull of gravity on a mass, found from weight = mass × gravitational field strength.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

Force is one of the most central ideas in A-Level mechanics. It underpins resultant force, free-body diagrams, [[Newton-Second-Law]], equilibrium of forces, moments and turning effects, and circular motion. A solid GCSE grasp of "force as an interaction with size and direction" is the prerequisite for the vector treatment used throughout A-Level.

## Everyday Intuition

Pushing a shopping trolley harder makes it speed up faster. A heavier trolley is harder to get moving with the same push. Friction from the ground slows it down once you stop pushing.

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level the everyday "push or pull" becomes a precise vector quantity. Forces are resolved into components, combined using vector addition, and represented on free-body diagrams. Force is tied to [[Acceleration]] through Newton's second law in the form F = ma, and extended into rates of change of [[Momentum]]. See [[From-Speed-to-Velocity]] and the A-Level [[Force]] physical-quantity page for the more formal treatment.
