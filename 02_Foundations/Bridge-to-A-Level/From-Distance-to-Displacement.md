# From Distance to Displacement

## Core Idea

At GCSE, "how far" usually means distance — the total length of path travelled. A-Level Physics needs displacement, which is the straight-line distance from start to finish *together with its direction*.

## Prior Knowledge Level

Bridge to A-Level

## Meaning

Distance is a scalar: it only adds up how far you have travelled, regardless of direction, and it never decreases. Displacement is a vector: it is the change in position, measured in a straight line from the starting point to the end point, with a direction.

The two can differ enormously. Walk 100 m east then 100 m back west: the distance travelled is 200 m, but the displacement is zero, because you finished where you started. Run one full lap of a 400 m track: distance is 400 m, displacement is zero.

This matters because the precise A-Level definitions of [[Velocity]] and [[Acceleration]] are built on displacement, not distance. Velocity is rate of change of *displacement*; average speed uses distance. Using distance where displacement is required (or vice versa) produces wrong answers and is a classic exam trap.

In one dimension, displacement is given a sign to show direction; in two dimensions it is a vector that can be resolved into components.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

Displacement underpins the SUVAT equations, motion graphs (the area under a velocity–time graph is displacement, not distance), projectile motion, oscillations and simple harmonic motion (displacement from equilibrium), and the displacement of particles in waves. Many marks are lost by mixing distance and displacement.

## Everyday Intuition

A satnav shows two numbers: the "as the crow flies" distance to your destination (closer to displacement) and the driving distance along the roads (the path distance). They are rarely the same. If you drive to a shop and back home, your displacement for the whole trip is zero even though you covered many miles.

## Common Misconceptions

- Using distance when displacement is required (or treating them as always equal)
- Forgetting that displacement can be zero even after a long journey
- Ignoring the direction/sign of displacement

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level, displacement becomes a signed or vector quantity used precisely in kinematics: it is the area under a velocity–time graph, the basis of the SUVAT equations, and the variable in simple harmonic motion and wave descriptions. The qualitative GCSE "how far" splits cleanly into path distance and vector displacement. See [[From-Speed-to-Velocity]] and the A-Level [[Velocity]] page.
