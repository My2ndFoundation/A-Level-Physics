# From Circuits to Circuit Models

## Core Idea

At GCSE, circuits are mostly described with rules of thumb ("current is the same in series", "voltage splits"). A-Level turns these into a consistent quantitative model built on conservation laws, so any circuit can be analysed, not just standard examples.

## Prior Knowledge Level

Bridge to A-Level

## Meaning

GCSE circuit work is largely qualitative and pattern-based: memorised behaviours for simple series and parallel arrangements. This is reliable for textbook circuits but does not explain *why* the rules work or how to handle a circuit that does not match a learned pattern.

The A-Level model is built from a few precise foundations:

- **Conservation of charge** gives Kirchhoff's first law: the total current into any junction equals the total current out. This is *why* current is shared at parallel junctions.
- **Conservation of energy** gives Kirchhoff's second law: around any loop, the total e.m.f. equals the sum of the potential differences. This is *why* voltages add up the way they do.
- **[[Ohms-Law]]** and resistor combination rules let you relate [[Voltage]], [[Electric-Current]] and [[Resistance]] anywhere in the circuit.

On top of this come ideas that GCSE leaves out or simplifies: a source has internal resistance, so terminal voltage falls under load; potential dividers split voltage in a controllable way; and components such as diodes and filament lamps are non-ohmic, so their I–V graphs are not straight lines.

The shift is from "remember what happens" to "model the circuit and calculate what must happen", grounded in conservation principles.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

This bridge supports the whole A-Level electricity topic: Kirchhoff's laws, series and parallel resistance derivations, internal resistance and e.m.f., potential dividers and sensor circuits, I–V characteristics, electrical power and energy, and capacitor charging. Without the modelling mindset, A-Level circuit questions become guesswork.

## Everyday Intuition

A phone charger and a kettle both run from the same mains, yet behave very differently — the model, not a memorised rule, tells you how current and voltage distribute. When many appliances are switched on at home, the supply voltage can dip slightly: that is internal-resistance behaviour the GCSE picture cannot explain but the A-Level model predicts.

## Common Misconceptions

- Treating series/parallel rules as magic facts rather than consequences of conservation laws
- Assuming the supply voltage stays constant whatever the load (ignoring internal resistance)
- Expecting every component to give a straight-line I–V graph

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level, the qualitative GCSE rules are derived from conservation of charge and energy via Kirchhoff's laws, combined with [[Ohms-Law]] and resistor-combination algebra, and extended with internal resistance, potential dividers and non-ohmic I–V analysis.
