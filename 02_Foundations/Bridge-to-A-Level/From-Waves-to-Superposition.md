# From Waves to Superposition

## Core Idea

GCSE describes a single wave and its properties. A-Level asks what happens when *two or more* waves meet at the same point — the principle of superposition — which explains interference, standing waves and [[Diffraction]] patterns.

## Prior Knowledge Level

Bridge to A-Level

## Meaning

GCSE wave work concentrates on one wave at a time: its wavelength, frequency, amplitude and speed, and the wave equation $v = f\lambda$. It rarely asks what happens where waves overlap.

The bridging idea is the **principle of superposition**: when two or more waves meet, the total displacement at any point is the *sum* of the displacements each wave would produce there on its own. After they pass through each other, each wave continues unchanged.

This single idea unlocks a whole class of A-Level phenomena:

- **Constructive interference** where waves arrive in step (in phase), reinforcing to give a larger amplitude.
- **Destructive interference** where waves arrive out of step (antiphase), partly or completely cancelling.
- **Standing waves**, formed when a wave and its reflection superpose, producing fixed nodes and antinodes.
- **Diffraction and interference patterns**, such as the fringes from a double slit, explained by the path difference between routes the waves take.

To use superposition quantitatively, GCSE wave description must be extended with the ideas of **phase**, **phase difference**, **path difference** and **coherence** (two sources keeping a constant phase relationship).

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

Superposition is the backbone of the A-Level waves topic: two-source interference, the double-slit and diffraction-grating experiments, single-slit [[Diffraction]], stationary waves on strings and in air columns, resonance, and the wave evidence for the nature of light. It also feeds into quantum ideas where particles show wave-like interference.

## Everyday Intuition

Two stones dropped into a pond send out ripples that cross: where two crests meet the water humps up higher, and where a crest meets a trough the surface barely moves. Noise-cancelling headphones add an "anti-noise" wave so that destructive interference flattens the unwanted sound — superposition put to practical use.

## Common Misconceptions

- Thinking two waves "collide" and stop, rather than passing through and continuing
- Believing waves must have equal amplitude to interfere
- Confusing amplitude addition (superposition) with energy simply doubling everywhere

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level, the single-wave GCSE description is extended with phase, phase difference, path difference and coherence, and the principle of [[Superposition]] is applied to interference, [[Diffraction]] and standing waves both qualitatively and quantitatively.
