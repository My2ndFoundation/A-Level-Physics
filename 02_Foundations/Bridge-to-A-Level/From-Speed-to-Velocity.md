# From Speed to Velocity

## Core Idea

GCSE work usually starts with speed, a single number telling you how fast something moves. A-Level Physics needs velocity, which carries direction as well as size, because in mechanics direction changes everything.

## Prior Knowledge Level

Bridge to A-Level

## Meaning

Speed is a scalar: it has only magnitude (for example, 30 m s⁻¹). Velocity is a vector: it has magnitude *and* direction (for example, 30 m s⁻¹ due east). Speed is the rate of change of distance; velocity is the rate of change of displacement.

This distinction matters because two motions with the same speed can be physically very different. A car driving round a roundabout at a steady 30 mph has constant speed but continuously changing velocity, because its direction keeps changing. A change in velocity — even with no change in speed — means there is an [[Acceleration]], and therefore (by [[Newton-Second-Law]]) a resultant [[Force]]. The GCSE picture, which treats speed as the whole story, cannot explain this; the velocity picture can.

At A-Level, signs are used to encode direction in one dimension (positive one way, negative the other), and vectors are resolved into components in two dimensions.

## Why It Matters for A-Level Physics

Velocity is the gateway quantity for almost all of A-Level mechanics: [[Acceleration]] as the rate of change of velocity, the SUVAT equations, projectile motion, [[Momentum]] (a vector), circular motion, and the velocity description of waves. Treating direction carelessly is one of the most common sources of lost marks.

## Everyday Intuition

A car going north at 30 mph and a car going south at 30 mph have the same speed but opposite velocities — and if they collide, that difference in direction is exactly what makes the crash so violent. Walking once around a running track at a steady pace: your speed is constant, but your velocity has been pointing in every direction, and you finish with zero displacement.

## Common Misconceptions

- Treating speed and velocity as interchangeable in all contexts
- Believing constant speed means zero acceleration (untrue when direction changes)
- Forgetting to assign and keep a consistent sign convention

## Bridge to A-Level

At A-Level the everyday "how fast" becomes a precise vector. Direction is handled with sign conventions in one dimension and component resolution in two dimensions; velocity is differentiated and integrated against time on motion graphs, and it feeds directly into [[Momentum]] and [[Newton-Second-Law]]. See the GCSE-layer pages and the A-Level [[Velocity]] physical-quantity page.
