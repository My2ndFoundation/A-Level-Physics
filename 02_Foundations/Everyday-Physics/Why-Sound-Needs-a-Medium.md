# Why Sound Needs a Medium

## Everyday Question

Why can't you hear an explosion in space, even though you can see its flash, and why does sound travel through air, water, and walls but not through a vacuum?

## Short Answer

Sound is a vibration that has to be passed from particle to particle. If there are no particles — as in the vacuum of space — there is nothing to carry the vibration, so there is no sound.

## Physical Explanation

When something makes a sound — a loudspeaker cone, a guitar string, vocal cords — it vibrates back and forth. As it pushes outward it squashes the air particles next to it together; as it moves back it lets them spread apart. Those particles bump into their neighbours, which bump into the next ones, and so on. A pattern of squashed (compressed) and spread-out (rarefied) regions travels outward. That travelling pattern is a sound wave.

Notice what actually moves: the *disturbance* travels outward, but the air particles themselves just vibrate back and forth around their starting positions — they do not stream from the speaker to your ear. Sound is the passing-on of a vibration through matter.

Because sound relies on particles to pass the vibration along, it must have a medium: a gas, liquid, or solid. It generally travels fastest in solids (particles are close together and tightly linked), slower in liquids, and slowest in gases.

Space is very nearly a vacuum — almost no particles. With nothing to compress and pass the vibration on, sound simply cannot travel. Light is different: it is an electromagnetic [[Waves]] and does not need particles, which is why the flash of an event in space reaches us but the bang never does.

## Common Wrong Intuition

A common wrong intuition is that air particles physically travel from the source all the way to your ear, like a wind. They do not — each particle only vibrates locally and passes the disturbance on. Another mistake is assuming all waves need a medium; electromagnetic waves such as light and radio travel happily through a vacuum.
