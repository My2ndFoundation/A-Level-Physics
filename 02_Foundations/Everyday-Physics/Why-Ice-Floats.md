# Why Ice Floats

## Everyday Question

Almost every solid sinks in its own liquid, so why does ice float on water — and why does this matter for life in lakes and ponds?

## Short Answer

Ice floats because it is less dense than liquid water. When water freezes, its molecules lock into an open structure that takes up more space, so the same mass of water becomes a larger volume of ice.

## Physical Explanation

Whether something floats depends on its density compared with the liquid around it. Density is how much mass is packed into a given volume. If an object is less dense than the liquid, the upward push from the liquid (upthrust) is enough to support it and it floats; if it is denser, it sinks.

For most substances, the solid is denser than the liquid because the particles pack more tightly when they freeze. Water is unusual. As liquid water cools toward freezing, its molecules begin to arrange into a regular, rather open lattice held together by the way water molecules bond to each other. This lattice has gaps in it, so the molecules are actually spaced *further apart* on average than in liquid water just above freezing.

Because the same number of molecules now occupies a larger volume, ice has a lower density than liquid water — only about 9% less, which is why roughly a tenth of a floating iceberg sits above the surface and the rest is hidden below.

This small quirk has huge consequences. When a pond freezes, the ice forms on top and floats, insulating the water beneath. Fish and other life can survive the winter in the liquid water below. If ice sank, ponds and lakes would freeze solid from the bottom up and life in them would be far harder.

## Common Wrong Intuition

A common wrong intuition is that ice floats because it is "lighter" than water. A large block of ice can weigh far more than a cup of water. Floating depends on density (mass per unit volume), not weight alone. Another mistake is assuming all solids sink in their liquids — water is a notable exception.
