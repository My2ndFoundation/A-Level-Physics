# Why Sky Is Blue

## Everyday Question

The Sun gives out white light, and the air is transparent, so why does the daytime sky look blue rather than colourless — and why do sunsets turn red?

## Short Answer

Sunlight is a mixture of all colours. The tiny gas molecules in the air scatter blue light much more strongly than red light, so blue light gets bounced all over the sky and reaches your eyes from every direction.

## Physical Explanation

White sunlight is made of a range of colours, each with its own wavelength. Red light has a longer wavelength; blue and violet light have shorter wavelengths.

As sunlight passes through the atmosphere, it meets enormous numbers of very small air molecules. These molecules scatter (redirect) light in all directions, but they do not treat all colours equally. Shorter-wavelength light — blue and violet — is scattered far more strongly than longer-wavelength red light. This selective scattering of short wavelengths is the key effect.

Because blue light is scattered in every direction by the whole sky, when you look up away from the Sun you see scattered blue light arriving from all over. The sky therefore looks blue. (It does not look violet, even though violet is scattered even more, partly because the Sun emits less violet and our eyes are less sensitive to it.)

At sunset the light reaches you after travelling a long, slanting path through the atmosphere. Along that long path most of the blue light has been scattered away in other directions before it can reach you. What is left to come straight through is mostly the longer wavelengths, so the Sun and sky near the horizon look orange and red.

## Common Wrong Intuition

A common wrong intuition is that the sky is blue because it reflects the blue sea, or because air is "naturally blue". Neither is true. The blue comes from selective scattering of short-wavelength sunlight by gas molecules; the sky is blue even far from any ocean.
