# Ray Model of Light

## Core Idea

The ray model represents light as straight lines (rays) showing the direction in which light energy travels, ignoring its wave and photon natures. Rays travel in straight lines in a uniform medium and change direction only by reflection or refraction at boundaries. This geometric picture is enough to predict image formation, the path of light through lenses and prisms, and the behaviour described by [[Snell-Law]] — without needing wavelength or phase.

## Assumptions

- Light travels in straight lines in a homogeneous medium.
- Direction changes only at surfaces (reflection, refraction).
- Wavelength is negligibly small compared with objects (no diffraction modelled).
- Rays are infinitely thin and carry only direction information.

## Quantities Involved

- Angle of incidence and refraction (degrees)
- Refractive index *n* (dimensionless)
- Speed of light in the medium *v* (m s⁻¹)

## Key Equations

- Law of reflection: angle in = angle out
- [[Snell-Law]]: $n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$
- $n = c/v$

## When to Use

Use it for mirrors, lenses, prisms, total internal reflection, optical fibres, and ray-tracing image construction — situations where the wavelength is tiny compared with the apparatus.

## Limits of the Model

It cannot explain diffraction, interference, or polarisation; for those the [[Wavefront-Model]] or full wave model is needed. It also fails when light passes apertures or obstacles comparable in size to its wavelength, where bending around edges occurs.

## Foundation Link

This formalises the everyday "light travels in straight lines / shadows have sharp edges" intuition into a predictive geometric tool.

## Common Mistakes

- Using ray optics where diffraction or interference dominates.
- Measuring angles from the surface instead of the normal.
- Forgetting refractive index depends on the medium pair.
