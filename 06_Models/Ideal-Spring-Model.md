# Ideal Spring Model

## Core Idea

The ideal spring model represents an elastic object as a massless component whose restoring force is exactly proportional to its extension or compression, always directed back toward the natural length. This is Hooke's law behaviour: *F = kx*, where *k* is the spring constant. The model removes complications of real springs (mass, internal friction, permanent stretching) so the spring acts purely as a linear store of [[Energy]].

## Assumptions

- Force is proportional to extension: *F = kx* (Hooke's law obeyed).
- The spring is massless.
- No energy is lost internally (perfectly elastic, no hysteresis).
- The spring does not exceed its limit of proportionality / elastic limit.

## Quantities Involved

- Extension *x* (m)
- Spring constant *k* (N m⁻¹)
- Restoring [[Force]] *F* (N, vector toward equilibrium)
- Elastic potential [[Energy]] *E* (J)

## Key Equations

- *F = kx*
- *E = ½kx²* (elastic potential energy = area under force–extension graph)
- Combined with mass: drives [[Simple-Harmonic-Oscillator]] motion.

## When to Use

Use it for springs, elastic bands (within the linear region), and as the restoring element in oscillation problems. It is also the conceptual basis for modelling interatomic bonds as tiny springs.

## Limits of the Model

It breaks down beyond the limit of proportionality (force no longer linear with extension), past the elastic limit (permanent deformation), and when the spring's own mass or damping is significant. Real materials also show hysteresis, which the ideal model ignores.

## Foundation Link

This sharpens the GCSE "stretchier springs stretch more" observation into the exact linear law *F = kx*, and introduces stored elastic energy as the area under a force–extension graph.

## Common Mistakes

- Applying *F = kx* beyond the limit of proportionality.
- Using *E = ½kx²* with the wrong (non-equilibrium) extension.
- Confusing spring constant *k* with stiffness of the material itself.
