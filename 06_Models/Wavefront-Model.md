# Wavefront Model

## Core Idea

The wavefront model represents a wave as a set of surfaces (wavefronts) joining all points oscillating in phase, moving outward at the wave speed. Rays are then simply lines perpendicular to the wavefronts. By treating every point on a wavefront as a source of secondary wavelets (Huygens' construction), the model explains how waves propagate, reflect, refract, diffract, and interfere — phenomena the [[Ray-Model-of-Light]] cannot handle. Plane waves have flat wavefronts; a point source produces spherical (circular) wavefronts.

## Assumptions

- Points on a wavefront are all in phase.
- Each point acts as a source of secondary spherical wavelets.
- The new wavefront is the envelope of these wavelets after a short time.
- The medium is uniform unless a boundary is reached.

## Quantities Involved

- Wavelength *λ* (m) — spacing between successive wavefronts
- Frequency *f* (Hz)
- Wave speed *v* (m s⁻¹)
- Phase (rad)

## Key Equations

- $v = f\lambda$
- [[Snell-Law]] explained via change of wavefront spacing on refraction
- Path difference → constructive/destructive interference

## When to Use

Use it to explain refraction (wavefronts bend because speed changes), diffraction at slits and edges, interference and superposition, and why optical fibres and lenses behave as they do at the wave level.

## Limits of the Model

It is a classical wave description: it does not capture the quantised, particle-like behaviour of light needed for the [[Photoelectric-Effect]] (use the [[Photon-Model]]). It also becomes cumbersome where simple geometry suffices.

## Foundation Link

This extends the everyday observation of ripples spreading on water into a general tool, and connects to the [[Ray-Model-of-Light]] by making rays perpendicular to wavefronts.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing wavefront spacing with amplitude.
- Forgetting wavefronts bend on refraction because speed (not frequency) changes.
- Treating wavefronts and rays as the same thing rather than perpendicular.
