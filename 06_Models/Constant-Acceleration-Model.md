# Constant Acceleration Model

## Core Idea

The constant acceleration model treats motion as having a fixed, unchanging [[Acceleration]] throughout the interval being studied. Under this assumption the relationships between displacement, [[Velocity]], acceleration and time become a small closed set of algebraic equations (the SUVAT equations). The model converts a messy real journey into a clean predictable one: if you know any three of the five quantities, the other two follow exactly. It is the workhorse model of A-Level kinematics because so many situations — a ball falling, a car braking evenly, a trolley on a slope — are well approximated by it over short intervals.

## Assumptions

- Acceleration is constant in both magnitude and direction.
- Motion is treated as one-dimensional (along a straight line) unless components are taken separately.
- Mass does not appear; only kinematic quantities are modelled.
- Air resistance and other variable forces are ignored unless explicitly included.
- The object is treated as a [[Point-Mass-Model|point mass]] (no rotation or size effects).

## Quantities Involved

- Displacement *s* (m, vector)
- Initial velocity *u* (m s⁻¹, vector)
- Final velocity *v* (m s⁻¹, vector)
- [[Acceleration]] *a* (m s⁻² , vector)
- Time *t* (s, scalar)

## Key Equations

- *v = u + at*
- *s = ut + ½at²*
- *v² = u² + 2as*
- *s = ½(u + v)t*
- See [[Using-SUVAT-Equations]] for selection strategy.

## When to Use

Use this model when a problem states "uniform acceleration", "constant acceleration", free fall near the Earth's surface (*a = g ≈ 9.81 m s⁻²*), or when a [[Velocity-Time-Graph]] is a straight line. A constant gradient on a velocity–time graph is the graphical signature of this model.

## Limits of the Model

It breaks down when the resultant force — and therefore acceleration — changes during the motion. Examples: motion with significant air resistance (acceleration falls as speed rises toward terminal velocity), spring or pendulum motion ([[Simple-Harmonic-Oscillator]] has continuously varying acceleration), and rocket flight where mass changes. In these cases SUVAT gives wrong answers and calculus or [[Finding-Area-Under-a-Graph|graphical area methods]] are needed instead.

## Foundation Link

This model extends the GCSE ideas of speed and acceleration into a precise vector framework. At GCSE acceleration is "change of speed per second"; here it becomes a constant vector linking four other vector/scalar quantities through exact equations.
