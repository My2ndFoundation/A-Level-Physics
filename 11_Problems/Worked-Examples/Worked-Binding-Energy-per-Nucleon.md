# Worked Binding Energy per Nucleon

## Problem

A helium-4 nucleus contains 2 protons and 2 neutrons. The mass of a proton is $1.00728\ \text{u}$, a neutron $1.00867\ \text{u}$, and the helium-4 nucleus $4.00150\ \text{u}$. Take $1\ \text{u} = 931.5\ \text{MeV}/c^2$. Find (a) the mass defect, (b) the total binding energy, and (c) the binding energy per nucleon.

## Solution

**(a) Mass defect.** Total mass of the separate nucleons:

$$m_{\text{nucleons}} = 2(1.00728) + 2(1.00867) = 2.01456 + 2.01734 = 4.03190\ \text{u}$$

Mass defect:

$$\Delta m = m_{\text{nucleons}} - m_{\text{nucleus}} = 4.03190 - 4.00150 = 0.03040\ \text{u}$$

**(b) Total binding energy.** Using $1\ \text{u} \leftrightarrow 931.5\ \text{MeV}$:

$$E_B = 0.03040 \times 931.5 = 28.3\ \text{MeV}$$

**(c) Binding energy per nucleon.** Helium-4 has 4 nucleons:

$$\frac{E_B}{A} = \frac{28.3}{4} = 7.1\ \text{MeV per nucleon}$$

## Unit Check

Mass defect in u; multiplying by the conversion $931.5\ \text{MeV}/\text{u}$ gives energy in MeV. Dividing by the dimensionless nucleon number $A$ keeps MeV per nucleon. Consistent.

## Key Insight

The bound nucleus is *less* massive than its separated nucleons; the missing mass has been released as binding energy ($E = \Delta m c^2$). Binding energy per nucleon measures nuclear stability — helium-4's high value ($\approx 7\ \text{MeV}$) explains why it is unusually stable.

## Common Mistakes

- Subtracting the masses the wrong way (nucleus must be lighter than the free nucleons).
- Forgetting to divide by the nucleon number for *per nucleon*.
- Dropping significant figures too early when the mass defect is a small difference of large numbers.
