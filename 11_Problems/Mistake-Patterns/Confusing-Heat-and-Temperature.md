# Confusing Heat and Temperature

## Mistake

Treating "heat" and "temperature" as the same thing — for example assuming that a hotter object always contains more thermal energy, or that supplying energy must always raise the temperature.

## Why It Happens

Everyday language uses "heat" loosely ("the heat of the fire"). [[Temperature]] measures the *average* random kinetic energy per particle, while heating is an energy *transfer* due to a temperature difference, and total thermal energy depends on mass and specific heat capacity as well as temperature. Because the words feel interchangeable, learners merge two distinct ideas.

## Example

A spark at 1000 °C lands on your hand and barely hurts; a cup of water at 80 °C scalds badly. The spark has higher temperature but tiny mass, so it transfers very little energy ($E = mc\Delta\theta$ with minuscule $m$). The water transfers far more energy despite a lower temperature.

A second case: while ice melts at 0 °C, energy is continuously supplied yet the temperature does not change at all — the energy goes into [[Specific-Latent-Heat]], raising the potential part of [[Internal-Energy]].

## Correct Approach

- Use [[Temperature]] for the average kinetic energy per particle (decides the *direction* of energy flow).
- Use energy transfer (heating) for the *amount* of energy moved; quantify it with $E = mc\Delta\theta$ ([[Specific-Heat-Capacity]]) within a state, or $E = mL$ ([[Specific-Latent-Heat]]) during a change of state.
- Remember that during a change of state, temperature stays constant even though energy is supplied.
