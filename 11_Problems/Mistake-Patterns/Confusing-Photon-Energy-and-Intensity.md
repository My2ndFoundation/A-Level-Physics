# Confusing Photon Energy and Intensity

## Mistake

Believing that brighter (more intense) light always means each photon carries
more energy.

## Why It Happens

Everyday language treats "bright" and "powerful" as the same. But photon
energy depends only on frequency ($E = hf$), while [[Intensity]] depends on how
many photons arrive per second per unit area.

## Example

A dim blue lamp and a bright red lamp shine on a metal. A student predicts the
bright red lamp ejects electrons because it is more intense. In the
[[Photoelectric-Effect]], emission depends on each photon's energy: if the red
photons have energy below the work function, no electrons are emitted no
matter how bright the lamp. The dim blue lamp, with higher-frequency photons,
can eject electrons.

## Correct Approach

Separate the two ideas: use $E = hf$ (or $E = hc/\lambda$) for the energy of one photon;
use [[Intensity]] for the rate of photon arrival. Brighter light = more
photons per second, not more energy per photon.

## Foundation Link

It builds on distinguishing "how much" from "how strong each one is" — amount
versus quality of energy.
