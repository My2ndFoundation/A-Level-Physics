# Forgetting Vector Direction

## Mistake

Adding or subtracting vector quantities as if they were plain numbers, ignoring that direction changes the result.

## Why It Happens

At GCSE many calculations only involve magnitudes, so learners build a habit of "just add the numbers." When direction first becomes essential, the habit persists and signs or angles are dropped.

## Example

A ball thrown upward at 6 m/s rebounds downward at 4 m/s after a wall bounce. A student writes the change in velocity as $4 - 6 = -2\ \text{m s}^{-1}$. Taking up as positive, the correct change is $(-4) - (+6) = -10\ \text{m s}^{-1}$: the directions must be carried through with signs.

## Correct Approach

Define a positive direction at the start. Assign signs (or use components) to every vector quantity. Combine vectors using components or a vector diagram, and only then take a magnitude if a scalar answer is required.

## Foundation Link

Builds on the GCSE idea that some quantities have direction (vectors) and others do not (scalars).
