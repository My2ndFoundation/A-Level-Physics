# Adding Forces as Scalars

## Mistake

Adding the magnitudes of forces together arithmetically, ignoring their
directions, when finding the [[Resultant-Force]].

## Why It Happens

Numbers are easy to add; arrows are not. Students treat "5 N and 5 N" as
"10 N" out of habit, forgetting that [[Force]] is a vector whose direction
changes the result.

## Example

Two forces of 5 N act on a point, one due east and one due north. A student
gives the resultant as 10 N. Correctly, the forces are perpendicular, so the
resultant magnitude is $\sqrt{5^2 + 5^2} \approx 7.1\ \text{N}$, directed $45^\circ$ north of east. Adding
them as scalars overstates the resultant and gives no direction at all.

## Correct Approach

Resolve each force into perpendicular components, add the components
separately, then recombine with Pythagoras and trigonometry. Only forces
acting along the same straight line may be added (or subtracted) directly.

## Foundation Link

It comes from not yet distinguishing scalars from vectors — the same root as
treating displacement like distance.
