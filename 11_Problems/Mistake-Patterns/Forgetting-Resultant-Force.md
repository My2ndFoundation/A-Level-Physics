# Forgetting Resultant Force

## Mistake

Using a single applied force in Newton's second law instead of the resultant (net) force, ignoring opposing forces such as friction, drag or weight components.

## Why It Happens

Introductory problems often feature one force on a frictionless surface, training learners to plug the first force they see into $F = ma$. Free-body thinking is skipped under exam pressure.

## Example

A 2 kg box is pushed with 10 N while friction of 4 N opposes it. A student computes $a = 10 / 2 = 5\ \text{m s}^{-2}$. The resultant force is $10 - 4 = 6\ \text{N}$, so the correct acceleration is $6 / 2 = 3\ \text{m s}^{-2}$.

## Correct Approach

Draw a free-body diagram and identify every force. Combine them (with direction) into a resultant. Substitute the resultant force, not an individual force, into Newton's second law.

## Foundation Link

Builds on the GCSE idea that an object's acceleration depends on the net force, and that balanced forces give no acceleration.
