# Confusing Angular and Linear Quantities

## Mistake

Treating angular and linear quantities as interchangeable: substituting linear speed v where [[Angular-Velocity]] ω is required (or vice versa), forgetting the factor of radius r, using degrees instead of radians, or confusing rev s⁻¹ with rad s⁻¹.

## Why It Happens

Both describe how fast something moves, and the symbols look similar. Learners forget that v = ωr links them through the radius, that the [[Radian]] is the required angular unit (one revolution = 2π rad, not 360 in formulae), and that ω = 2πf, not f.

## Example

A wheel of radius 0.25 m turns at 4.0 rev s⁻¹. A common error is to write a = v²/r with v = 4.0, giving a wrong answer. Correct: ω = 2π × 4.0 = 25.1 rad s⁻¹, so rim speed v = ωr = 6.3 m s⁻¹ and [[Centripetal-Acceleration]] a = ω²r = 158 m s⁻². Another frequent error: leaving a calculator in degree mode when evaluating x = A cos(ωt) in [[Simple-Harmonic-Motion]].

## Correct Approach

- Identify whether the quantity is angular (θ, ω, in rad, rad s⁻¹) or linear (s, v, in m, m s⁻¹).
- Always convert revolutions and frequency to angular measure: ω = 2πf = 2π/T; one revolution = 2π rad.
- Use v = ωr and a = ω²r = v²/r to move between them, never dropping the radius.
- Keep the calculator in radian mode for [[Simple-Harmonic-Motion-Equation]] work.

## Foundation Link

Rooted in the GCSE habit of using only linear [[Speed]] and measuring angles in degrees, before the [[Radian]] is introduced as the natural unit for rotation and oscillation.
