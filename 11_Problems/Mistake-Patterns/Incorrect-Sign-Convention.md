# Incorrect Sign Convention

## Mistake

Choosing or applying a sign convention inconsistently — mixing positive directions partway through, or dropping a negative sign for a quantity that opposes the chosen positive direction.

## Why It Happens

Sign conventions are arbitrary but must be consistent. Learners pick a direction implicitly, never write it down, then unconsciously switch it when a quantity "feels" negative.

## Example

A ball is thrown up and caught. Taking up as positive, a student uses displacement $+5\ \text{m}$, initial velocity $+12\ \text{m s}^{-1}$, but acceleration $+9.8\ \text{m s}^{-2}$ instead of $-9.8\ \text{m s}^{-2}$. Gravity acts downward, so with up positive the acceleration must be negative; the inconsistent sign gives an impossible answer.

## Correct Approach

State the positive direction explicitly at the start. Assign signs to every vector quantity consistently with that choice and keep it fixed for the whole problem. Sanity-check that the final sign matches the expected physical direction.

## Foundation Link

Builds on the GCSE idea that direction can be represented by positive and negative signs along a line.
