# Confusing Mass and Weight

## Mistake

Treating mass and weight as the same physical quantity — quoting a "weight" in kilograms, or assuming an object's weight is unchanged when it is moved somewhere with a different gravitational field strength.

## Why It Happens

In everyday language "weight" is used loosely for what physics calls mass. Bathroom scales display kilograms even though they respond to a force, which reinforces the confusion. Because mass and weight are proportional in a fixed location, the difference rarely matters in daily life, so the distinction is never reinforced.

## Example

A student writes that an astronaut "weighs 80 kg on the Moon." Mass is the same 80 kg everywhere, but the Moon's gravitational field strength is about one sixth of Earth's, so the astronaut's *weight* (a force) is roughly one sixth of its Earth value. The kilogram answer describes mass, not weight.

## Correct Approach

State mass in kilograms and weight in newtons. Compute weight from $W = mg$, where `g` is the local gravitational field strength. Mass is an invariant property of the object; weight is the gravitational force acting on it and depends on location. When a problem says "weight," expect a force in newtons.

## Foundation Link

This builds on the GCSE distinction between mass as the amount of matter and weight as the pull of gravity, and on the idea of gravitational field strength as force per unit mass.
