# Interpret a Velocity Time Graph

## Problem Signal

The question provides a velocity–time graph (or a description of motion to sketch one) and asks for acceleration, distance/displacement, or a description of the motion in stages. Trigger phrases: "use the graph to find the acceleration", "calculate the total distance travelled", "describe the motion between A and B", or "sketch the corresponding displacement–time graph".

## Required Quantities

- [[Velocity]]
- [[Acceleration]]
- [[Displacement]]

## Required Concepts

- Gradient as rate of change
- Area under a curve as accumulated quantity

## Required Laws or Results

- $a = \Delta v / \Delta t$ (gradient)
- $s = $ area under the graph

## Required Methods

- [[Velocity-Time-Graph]]

## Standard Approach

1. Read the axes and units; confirm it is velocity (not speed or displacement) versus time.
2. For acceleration in a stage, find the gradient: $a = \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$ over that section.
3. For distance/displacement, find the area under the line: split into triangles, rectangles and trapezia.
4. Distinguish displacement (signed area; below the axis is negative) from distance (sum of magnitudes).
5. Describe motion stage by stage: constant velocity (horizontal line), uniform acceleration (straight slope), deceleration (slope toward axis).
6. State units explicitly for each result.

## Variations

- Curved graph: gradient by drawing a tangent; area by counting squares or trapezium estimation.
- Object reversing direction: velocity crosses the time axis, so displacement and distance differ.
- Two objects on the same axes: they have equal velocity where lines cross, equal displacement where areas match.

## Common Traps

- [[Confusing-Mass-and-Weight]]
- Confusing a velocity–time graph with a displacement–time graph (gradient meanings differ).
- Reporting distance when displacement is required (or vice versa) when motion reverses.
- Forgetting that area below the time axis represents negative displacement.
