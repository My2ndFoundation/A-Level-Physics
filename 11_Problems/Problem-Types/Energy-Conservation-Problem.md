# Energy Conservation Problem

## Problem Signal

A body changes height and/or speed and the question links these through energy rather than forces and time. Trigger phrases: "find the speed at the bottom", "how high does it rise", "energy lost to friction", "efficiency of the system", "power output". Choose energy methods when force/time data is missing but start/end states are known.

## Required Quantities

- [[Kinetic-Energy]]
- [[Gravitational-Potential-Energy]]
- [[Power]]

## Required Concepts

- Conservation of energy
- Work done by/against friction

## Required Laws or Results

- $E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$, $\Delta E_p = mg\Delta h$
- Work–energy principle; efficiency $= \dfrac{\text{useful output}}{\text{total input}}$

## Required Methods

- [[Applying-Conservation-of-Energy]]

## Standard Approach

1. Identify the start and end states and list which energy stores are present in each.
2. Write the energy balance: total energy in = total energy out + energy dissipated.
3. For frictionless cases, equate $\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ and $mg\Delta h$ transfers directly.
4. For dissipative cases, include work done against friction $= F_{\text{fric}} \times d$.
5. Solve for the unknown speed, height, or dissipated energy.
6. For power, divide energy transferred by time, or use $P = Fv$.

## Variations

- Pendulum / rollercoaster: interchange of $E_k$ and $E_p$ along a path.
- Spring systems: include elastic potential energy $\tfrac{1}{2}kx^2$.
- Efficiency problems comparing useful and wasted energy transfers.

## Common Traps

- Double-counting an energy store (e.g. including both work done by gravity and $\Delta E_p$).
- Using mass in grams or height in cm.
- Forgetting that friction removes energy but does not "store" it usefully.
- Assuming energy is conserved as useful energy when some is dissipated.
