# Amplitude

## Core Idea

Amplitude is the size of an oscillation — the maximum displacement from the equilibrium (rest) position. A loud sound has a large pressure amplitude; a bright light has a large electric-field amplitude. It controls how much energy a wave or oscillation carries, not how fast it repeats.

## Symbol

`A` (sometimes `x₀` or `a`)

## SI Unit

Depends on the oscillating quantity: `m` for a displacement wave, `Pa` for a sound-pressure wave, `V m⁻¹` for an electromagnetic wave.

## Scalar or Vector

Scalar magnitude (the maximum value of a varying displacement, which is itself a vector).

## Definition

Amplitude is the maximum magnitude of displacement of an oscillating system from its equilibrium position.

## Related Equations

- For SHM: displacement `x = A cos(ωt)` or `x = A sin(ωt)` — `A` = amplitude, `ω` = angular frequency (rad s⁻¹), `t` = time (s).
- Maximum speed in SHM: `v_max = ωA` (m s⁻¹).
- Maximum acceleration: `a_max = ω²A` (m s⁻²).
- Intensity (and energy) ∝ amplitude²: `I ∝ A²`. See [[Intensity]].

## How It Is Measured

Read the peak value on an oscilloscope or data-logger trace, or measure the maximum displacement directly (e.g. with a scale behind an oscillating mass or a CRO for a sound signal). Averaging over several cycles improves reliability.

## Graphical Meaning

On a displacement–time (or displacement–position) graph, the amplitude is the **distance from the equilibrium line to a peak** (half the peak-to-trough height). Damped oscillations show an amplitude that decreases over time.

## Common Mistakes

- Taking the full peak-to-trough distance as the amplitude (it is half of that)
- Confusing amplitude with wavelength
- Assuming amplitude affects frequency or period (it does not, for SHM)
