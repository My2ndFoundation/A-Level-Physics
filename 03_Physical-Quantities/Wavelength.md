# Wavelength

## Core Idea

Wavelength is the length of one complete wave — the distance from one crest to the next, or between any two points in the same phase. It sets the "size" of a wave: long radio waves are metres across, while X-rays are smaller than an atom. It controls how strongly waves diffract and where interference fringes appear.

## Symbol

`λ` (Greek lambda)

## SI Unit

`m` (metre)

## Scalar or Vector

Scalar. Magnitude only; positive.

## Definition

Wavelength is the shortest distance between two points on a wave that are oscillating in phase (e.g. crest to adjacent crest).

## Related Equations

- $v = f\lambda$ — `v` = wave speed (m s⁻¹), `f` = frequency (Hz), `λ` = wavelength (m).
- Double-slit fringe spacing: $w = \lambda D / s$ — `w` = fringe separation (m), `D` = slit-to-screen distance (m), `s` = slit separation (m).
- Diffraction grating: $d\sin\theta = n\lambda$ — `d` = slit spacing (m), `n` = order, `θ` = diffraction angle.
- de Broglie: $\lambda = h/p$ (frontier orientation).

## How It Is Measured

For light: a double-slit or diffraction grating with measured fringe geometry, rearranged for `λ`. For sound or microwaves: locate adjacent nodes/antinodes of a standing wave and measure the spacing (half a wavelength). For water/wave-tank waves: measure directly with a scale or via stroboscopic imaging.

## Graphical Meaning

On a displacement–position ("snapshot") graph, wavelength is the distance for one full cycle along the x-axis. (On a displacement–time graph it is the period, not the wavelength, that appears.)
