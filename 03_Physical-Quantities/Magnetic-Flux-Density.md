# Magnetic Flux Density

## Core Idea

Magnetic flux density measures the strength of a magnetic field ‚ÄĒ how much force the field exerts on a current-carrying wire or a moving charge. It is what we usually mean informally by "how strong the magnet is". One tesla is a very strong field; the Earth's field is only about 50 ¬ĶT.

## Symbol

`B`

## SI Unit

`T` (tesla). `1 T = 1 N A‚ĀĽ¬Ļ m‚ĀĽ¬Ļ = 1 Wb m‚ĀĽ¬≤`.

## Scalar or Vector

Vector. Its direction is the direction a free north pole would point (along the field lines).

## Definition

Magnetic flux density is defined through the force law: it is the force per unit current per unit length on a conductor placed perpendicular to the field.

## Related Equations

- `F = BIL` ‚ÄĒ `F` = force (N), `B` = flux density (T), `I` = current (A), `L` = length of conductor in the field (m); conductor perpendicular to `B`.
- `F = BIL sinőł` for a conductor at angle `őł` to the field.
- Force on a moving charge: `F = Bqv` ‚ÄĒ `q` = charge (C), `v` = speed (m s‚ĀĽ¬Ļ).
- Magnetic flux: `ő¶ = BA` ‚ÄĒ `A` = area (m¬≤), `ő¶` in webers (Wb).

## How It Is Measured

A current-balance: a known current `I` flows through a wire of length `L` in the field; the magnetic force is measured by the change in reading of a top-pan balance, giving `B = F/(IL)`. Alternatively a calibrated Hall probe reads `B` directly.

## Graphical Meaning

A graph of force against current for a fixed wire length in a uniform field is a straight line through the origin with gradient `BL`; dividing the gradient by `L` gives `B`.
