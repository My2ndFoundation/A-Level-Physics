# Force

## Core Idea

A force is a push or a pull. Forces change an object's motion (speed up, slow down, change direction) or deform it. At A-Level the key idea is the **resultant** (net) force: it is the resultant, not any single force, that determines acceleration.

> This is the A-Level **physical-quantity** page. For the prerequisite idea, see the GCSE-layer foundation page under Foundation Links.

## Symbol

`F`

## SI Unit

`N` (newton). `1 N = 1 kg m s⁻²` — the force giving a 1 kg mass an acceleration of 1 m s⁻².

## Scalar or Vector

Vector. Forces add as vectors; equilibrium requires the resultant force (and resultant moment) to be zero.

## Definition

A force is an interaction that, when unopposed, changes the motion of an object. Quantitatively, the resultant force equals the rate of change of momentum (Newton's second law).

## Related Equations

- $F = ma$ — `F` = resultant force (N), `m` = mass (kg), `a` = acceleration (m s⁻²). See [[Newton-Second-Law]].
- $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$ — `Δp` = change in momentum (kg m s⁻¹), `Δt` = time (s).
- Weight: $W = mg$ — `g` = gravitational field strength (N kg⁻¹). See [[Weight]].
- Spring: $F = kx$ — `k` = spring constant (N m⁻¹), `x` = extension (m). See [[Hookes-Law]].
- Gravitation: $F = GMm/r^2$ — see [[Newtons-Law-of-Gravitation]]. Electrostatic: see [[Coulombs-Law]].

## How It Is Measured

Newtonmeters (calibrated springs), force sensors, or indirectly via $F = ma$ (measured mass and acceleration) or via measured extension and a known spring constant.

## Graphical Meaning

On a [[Force-Extension-Graph]], gradient = spring constant and area = elastic strain energy. On a force–time graph, the area = impulse (= change in momentum).

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting force is a vector (ignoring direction in resultants)
- Confusing mass and weight
- Assuming motion requires a net force (it requires only a net force to *change* motion)
