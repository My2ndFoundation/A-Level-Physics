# Young Modulus

## Core Idea

The Young modulus measures how stiff a material is — how much stress is needed to produce a given strain. It is a property of the *material*, not of the particular sample: steel always has roughly the same Young modulus whether it is a wire or a girder. A high Young modulus means a stiff material that is hard to stretch.

## Symbol

`E`

## SI Unit

`Pa` (pascal) `= N m⁻²`; typically quoted in GPa (e.g. steel ≈ 200 GPa).

## Scalar or Vector

Scalar. Magnitude only; a positive material constant.

## Definition

The Young modulus is the ratio of tensile stress to tensile strain in the region where the material obeys Hooke's law (the linear, elastic region).

## Related Equations

- `E = σ / ε` — `E` = Young modulus (Pa), `σ` = stress (Pa), `ε` = strain (no unit).
- Expanded: `E = (F L) / (A x)` — `F` = force (N), `L` = original length (m), `A` = cross-sectional area (m²), `x` = extension (m).
- `A = πd²/4` for a wire of diameter `d` (m).

## How It Is Measured

The standard [[Measuring-Young-Modulus]] experiment: a long thin wire is loaded with known masses; extension is measured against a fixed scale; diameter is measured with a micrometer. A graph of stress against strain (or force against extension) is plotted and the Young modulus obtained from the gradient of the straight-line region.

## Graphical Meaning

On a [[Stress-Strain-Graph]], the Young modulus is the **gradient of the initial straight-line portion**. Equivalently, on a [[Force-Extension-Graph]], gradient `= EA/L`. A steeper line means a stiffer material.
