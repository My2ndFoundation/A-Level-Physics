# Specific Heat Capacity

## Core Idea

Specific heat capacity is the energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1 °C) without changing its state.

## Symbol

- $c$

## SI Unit

- J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ (equivalently J kg⁻¹ °C⁻¹, since a change of 1 K equals a change of 1 °C)

## Scalar or Vector

- Scalar

## Definition

The specific heat capacity of a substance is the energy required per unit mass to produce a unit rise in temperature, with no change of state.

## Related Equations

$$ \Delta E = m c \Delta\theta $$

where $\Delta E$ is energy transferred (J), $m$ is mass (kg), $c$ is specific heat capacity (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹) and $\Delta\theta$ is the temperature change (K or °C). Valid only when there is no change of state and (ideally) no energy lost to surroundings.

## How It Is Measured

By electrical heating: a known electrical energy $E = VIt$ is supplied to a measured mass $m$ and the temperature rise $\Delta\theta$ recorded, giving $c = \dfrac{VIt}{m\,\Delta\theta}$. See [[Measuring-Specific-Heat-Capacity]]. The method of mixtures is an alternative.

## Graphical Meaning

Plot total energy supplied $E$ (y-axis) against temperature $\theta$ (x-axis) for known mass $m$. Within a single state the graph is a straight line of gradient $mc$, so $c = \dfrac{\text{gradient}}{m}$. Plateaus (zero gradient) correspond to changes of state, where energy goes to [[Specific-Latent-Heat]] instead.
