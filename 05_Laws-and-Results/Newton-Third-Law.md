# Newton Third Law

## Statement

When object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts an equal-magnitude, opposite-direction force on object A. These two forces are of the same type, act along the same line, and act on **different objects**.

## Equation

`F_{A on B} = − F_{B on A}`

The two forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

## Symbols and Units

- `F_{A on B}`: force exerted by A on B, newtons `N` (vector)
- `F_{B on A}`: force exerted by B on A, newtons `N` (vector)

## Conditions

- The pair acts on **two different bodies** — never on the same body.
- Both forces are of the **same type** (e.g. both gravitational, or both contact, or both electromagnetic).
- Valid for speeds well below light speed; modified by field-delay effects at relativistic scales.

## Physical Meaning

Forces always occur in pairs because a force is an interaction between two objects. A foot pushes back on the ground; the ground pushes forward on the foot, which is what drives walking. Because the paired forces act on *different* bodies, they do **not** cancel out — only forces on the *same* body can be combined into a resultant. This is the key to avoiding the classic "the forces cancel so nothing moves" error.

## Foundation Link

GCSE introduces "every action has an equal and opposite reaction". A-Level adds the crucial precision that the two forces act on different objects and are the same type of force, which is why a book on a table is *not* a third-law pair with its weight (those act on the same body and are an equilibrium pair via the first law instead).

## How to Use

1. Identify the two interacting objects.
2. Name each force as "X on Y".
3. The reaction is "Y on X" — same size, opposite direction, same type.
4. Apply [[Newton-Second-Law]] to each body separately, not to the pair.

## Derivation or Explanation

Newton's third law is equivalent to [[Conservation-of-Momentum]] for an isolated system: equal and opposite forces over the same time produce equal and opposite impulses, so total momentum stays constant.
