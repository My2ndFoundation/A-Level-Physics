# Law of Reflection

## Statement

When a wave reflects from a boundary, the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection (both measured from the normal), and the incident ray, the reflected ray and the normal all lie in the same plane.

## Equation

$$\theta_i = \theta_r$$

## Symbols and Units

- Symbol: $\theta_i$ — Meaning: angle of incidence (between incident ray and the normal) — Unit: degrees `°` (or radians `rad`)
- Symbol: $\theta_r$ — Meaning: angle of reflection (between reflected ray and the normal) — Unit: degrees `°` (or radians `rad`)

The **normal** is the line perpendicular to the surface at the point where the ray meets it. Both angles are always measured from this normal, never from the surface.

## Conditions

- Applies to all waves at a boundary — light, sound, water and seismic waves.
- Holds for specular (mirror-like) reflection from a smooth surface. A rough surface gives diffuse reflection: the law still holds at each microscopic facet, but the surface as a whole scatters the wave.
- Frequency, [[Wavelength]] and [[Wave-Speed]] are unchanged on reflection because the wave stays in the same medium.

## Physical Meaning

The law is a geometric consequence of the wave keeping a constant speed in the medium: each part of an incoming [[Wave-Refraction|wavefront]] travels the same extra distance before and after striking the boundary, so the reflected wavefront leaves symmetrically about the normal. A possible phase change on reflection (inversion at a denser/fixed boundary) does not affect the angles but matters for [[Standing-Waves]] and [[Interference]].

## Foundation Link

Builds on the GCSE idea that "light bounces off a mirror" and the ray-box demonstration that the bounce is symmetric about the normal. A-Level makes the angle measurement precise (from the normal) and connects it to the [[Ray-Model-of-Light]] and to wavefront reasoning.

## How to Use

1. Draw the normal at the point of incidence.
2. Measure the angle of incidence from the normal (not the surface).
3. Set the angle of reflection equal to it on the opposite side of the normal.
4. For multiple reflections (e.g. a periscope or corner reflector), apply the law at each surface in turn.

## Derivation or Explanation

Using Huygens' wavefront construction: every point on the wavefront reaching the boundary acts as a source of secondary wavelets travelling at the same speed `v`. The envelope of the reflected wavelets makes an angle with the boundary equal to that of the incident wavefront, which gives $\theta_i = \theta_r$ when re-expressed relative to the normal.

## Applications

- Plane mirrors, periscopes and corner reflectors.
- Ultrasound and radar ranging; sonar echo location.
- [[Total-Internal-Reflection]] in optical fibres (a limiting case at a boundary).

## Frontier Links

- The same boundary reasoning generalises to reflection of matter waves and photons, sketched in [[Quantum-Mechanics-Map]].

## Common Mistakes

- [[Wave-Reflection|Measuring angles from the surface instead of from the normal]].
- Thinking reflection changes the wave's frequency or speed.
- Confusing specular and diffuse reflection (the law still holds locally for diffuse).
