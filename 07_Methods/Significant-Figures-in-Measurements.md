# Significant Figures in Measurements

## Purpose

To record and report measured and calculated values with a number of digits that honestly reflects their [[Measurement-Uncertainty|uncertainty]].

## When to Use

Every time data is tabulated (see [[Results-Table]]) or a final answer is quoted.

## Prerequisites

- [[Measurement-Uncertainty]]
- [[Calculating-Percentage-Uncertainty]]

## Method

1. **Raw readings** are recorded to the resolution of the instrument, and **all readings of the same quantity to the same number of decimal places**.
2. A **calculated result** should be quoted to the same number of significant figures as the *least precise* measurement it was derived from (no calculation can add precision that the data lack).
3. **Mean values** may be quoted to **one more significant figure** than the individual readings.
4. The uncertainty is normally quoted to **1 significant figure**, and the value rounded to the same decimal place as its uncertainty.
5. Watch leading zeros (not significant), trailing zeros after a decimal point (significant), and use standard form to make the count unambiguous.

## Worked Example

Readings 4.2, 4.3, 4.2 cm (2 s.f. each) give a mean of 4.233… cm. By the mean rule this is quoted as `4.23 cm` (3 s.f.). If the uncertainty is `± 0.1 cm`, report `4.2 ± 0.1 cm`.

## Why It Works

Significant figures are a coarse shorthand for uncertainty: extra digits claim a precision the measurement does not have, and too few discard real information.

## Common Mistakes

- Copying a calculator's full display into a final answer.
- Mismatching the decimal places of a value and its uncertainty.
