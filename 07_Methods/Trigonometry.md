# Trigonometry

> Deliberately shallow **cross-subject orientation** page — how a Maths tool is used in A-Level Physics, not a full Maths lesson.

## Purpose

Trigonometry relates the angles and sides of triangles via sine, cosine and tangent. In physics it is the engine behind **resolving vectors** into components and analysing anything periodic (waves, oscillations, circular motion).

## When to Use

Whenever a vector acts at an angle, a force is on a slope, or a quantity varies sinusoidally with time or position.

## Prerequisites

- [[Vectors-and-Scalars]]
- [[Vector-Triangle]]

## Method

1. Draw the right-angled triangle for the vector or geometry.
2. Apply $\text{component} = \text{magnitude} \times \cos\theta$ (adjacent) or $\times \sin\theta$ (opposite).
3. Recombine with Pythagoras and $\tan\theta$ for the resultant and its direction.

## Worked Example

A `20 N` force at `30°` to the horizontal has horizontal component $20 \cos 30° \approx 17.3 \text{ N}$ and vertical $20 \sin 30° = 10 \text{ N}$. See [[Resolving-Forces]], [[Resolving-Vectors]].

## Why It Works

Similar right-angled triangles have fixed side ratios; sin, cos and tan name those ratios for a given angle.

## Common Mistakes

- Mixing up which component uses sin vs cos (define the angle carefully).
- Calculator in the wrong angle mode (degrees vs radians).
