# Integration

> Deliberately shallow **cross-subject orientation** page — how a Maths tool is used in A-Level Physics, not a full Maths lesson.

## Purpose

Integration accumulates a quantity over an interval — the reverse of [[Differentiation]]. In physics it is the **area under a graph**: area under velocity–time = displacement; area under force–displacement = work; area under force–time = impulse.

## When to Use

When a question asks for a total accumulated over time or distance, or explicitly for the **area under a curve**.

## Prerequisites

- [[Finding-Area-Under-a-Graph]]
- [[Differentiation]]

## Method

1. Identify the function to accumulate, e.g. velocity `v(t)`.
2. Integrate over the interval: displacement `s = ∫v dt`.
3. On a graph, evaluate the **area between the curve and the axis** (count signed areas).

## Worked Example

For constant `v = 12 m s⁻¹` over `5 s`, `s = ∫v dt = 12 × 5 = 60 m` — the rectangle's area. See [[Velocity-Time-Graph]], [[Work]], [[Impulse]].

## Why It Works

The definite integral is the limit of summing thin strips (Riemann sum) — exactly the area under the curve.

## Common Mistakes

- Ignoring that area below the axis is negative.
- Forgetting unit consistency (axes' units multiply to give the area's unit).
