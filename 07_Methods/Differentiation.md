# Differentiation

> Deliberately shallow **cross-subject orientation** page — it shows how a Maths tool is used in A-Level Physics, not a full Maths lesson.

## Purpose

Differentiation finds an **instantaneous rate of change** — how fast one quantity changes with respect to another. In physics it turns a quantity into its rate: position → velocity, velocity → acceleration, charge → current.

## When to Use

When a question asks for a value "at an instant", for the **gradient of a curved graph**, or for a maximum/minimum (where the derivative is zero).

## Prerequisites

- [[Finding-Gradient-from-a-Graph]]
- [[Vectors-and-Scalars]]

## Method

1. Identify the function, e.g. displacement `s(t)`.
2. Differentiate with respect to the variable: $v = \frac{ds}{dt}$, $a = \frac{dv}{dt}$.
3. For graph work, the derivative is the **tangent gradient** at that point.

## Worked Example

For $s = 5t^2$, velocity $v = \frac{ds}{dt} = 10t$; at $t = 3 \text{ s}$, $v = 30 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. See [[Velocity]] and [[Acceleration]].

## Why It Works

A derivative is the limit of the gradient of a chord as the interval shrinks to zero — the exact slope of the tangent.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing average rate (chord) with instantaneous rate (tangent).
- Forgetting that velocity and acceleration are vectors — sign carries direction.
