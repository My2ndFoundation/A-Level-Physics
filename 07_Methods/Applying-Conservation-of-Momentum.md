# Applying Conservation of Momentum

## Purpose

This method uses [[Conservation-of-Momentum]] to solve collision and explosion problems by setting total [[Momentum]] before an interaction equal to total momentum after it, provided no external resultant force acts.

## When to Use

Use it for collisions (elastic or inelastic), explosions, recoil, and any interaction between bodies where external forces are negligible during the brief interaction. It is especially useful when forces during contact are unknown.

## Prerequisites

- [[Momentum]]
- [[Conservation-of-Momentum]]

## Method

1. Define the system (all interacting bodies) and a positive direction.
2. Write each body's momentum before the interaction: $p = mv$ with correct signs.
3. Write each body's momentum after the interaction.
4. Set total momentum before = total momentum after.
5. Solve for the unknown velocity or mass.
6. For energy questions, separately check whether kinetic energy is conserved (elastic) or not (inelastic).

## Worked Example

A 2.0 kg trolley moving at 3.0 m s⁻¹ collides with a stationary 1.0 kg trolley and they stick together. Before: total momentum $= 2.0 \times 3.0 + 0 = 6.0 \text{ kg m s}^{-1}$. After: $(2.0 + 1.0)v = 6.0$, so $v = 2.0 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. Kinetic energy falls ($9.0 \text{ J} \rightarrow 6.0 \text{ J}$), confirming an inelastic collision.

## Why It Works

By [[Newton-Second-Law]] and Newton's third law, the internal forces between colliding bodies are equal and opposite, so they produce equal and opposite momentum changes that cancel. With no external resultant force, total momentum is unchanged.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting momentum is a vector — dropping signs/direction.
- Assuming kinetic energy is conserved in an inelastic collision.
- Omitting a body from the system.
