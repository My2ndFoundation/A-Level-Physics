# Drawing Free Body Diagrams

## Purpose

A free body diagram isolates one object and shows every external [[Force]] acting on it as a labelled arrow. It is the essential first step before applying [[Newton-Second-Law]] or [[Resolving-Forces]], because it makes the resultant force unambiguous.

## When to Use

Use it at the start of nearly every dynamics or statics problem: objects on slopes, hanging masses, connected bodies, lifts, and anything where you need the net force or equilibrium condition.

## Prerequisites

- [[Force]]
- [[Free-Body-Model]]

## Method

1. Choose the single body to analyse and represent it as a dot or simple box.
2. Identify all forces acting *on* it: weight (always, downward), normal contact force, tension, friction, drag, applied/push forces.
3. Draw each force as an arrow from the body, pointing in its actual direction, length roughly proportional to magnitude.
4. Label each arrow with a symbol or value.
5. Exclude forces the body exerts on other objects (third-law partners act elsewhere).
6. For connected bodies, draw a separate diagram for each.

## Worked Example

A book resting on a table: draw the book as a box, weight *W* arrow pointing down, normal contact force *N* arrow pointing up from the table surface. Since the book is in equilibrium, *N = W*. The book's downward push on the table is *not* drawn — it acts on the table, not the book.

## Why It Works

[[Newton-Second-Law]] applies to a single body and its resultant force. Restricting the diagram to forces *on* the chosen body guarantees the vector sum of the arrows equals *ma* for that body.

## Common Mistakes

- [[Free-Body-Model]] misuse: including forces the body exerts on others.
- Drawing both members of an action–reaction pair on one body.
- Omitting weight or the normal contact force.
