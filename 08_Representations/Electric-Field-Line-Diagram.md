# Electric-Field-Line Diagram

## Core Idea

An electric-field-line diagram represents an electric field by drawing lines whose direction gives the direction of the force on a small positive test charge and whose density represents the [[Electric-Field-Strength]].

## Form

Arrows/lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges (or run from a positive plate to a negative plate). For a point charge the lines are radial; for two parallel charged plates the lines are uniform, equally spaced and perpendicular to the plates (except for edge fringing). Lines never cross, because the field has a single direction at each point.

## Axes / Labels / Components

Not a graph. Components:

- Field lines with arrows pointing away from positive, toward negative charge.
- Source charges or charged plates, labelled with sign.
- Line spacing: closer lines = stronger field.
- (Optional) equipotential lines drawn perpendicular to the field lines.

## Physical Meaning

The pattern shows both the direction of the electrostatic force on a positive charge (tangent to the line) and the relative strength (line density). A radial pattern indicates an inverse-square point-charge field; uniformly spaced parallel lines indicate a uniform field, as between capacitor plates where $E = V/d$.

## Gradient / Area / Intercepts

Not applicable to the diagram itself. The associated relationships are $E = F/Q$ (force per unit charge) and, for a uniform field, $E = V/d$.

## Converts To / From

- From: a charge configuration or a charged-plate arrangement.
- To: [[Electric-Field-Strength]] estimates from line density, and equipotential maps.

## Related Quantities

- [[Electric-Field-Strength]]

## Related Methods

- Comparing line density to compare field strength
- Relating $E = V/d$ for a uniform field

## Common Mistakes

- Drawing field lines that cross (the field cannot have two directions at one point).
- Reversing the convention — lines point in the direction of force on a *positive* charge.
- Forgetting that uniform field lines stay parallel and equally spaced.
