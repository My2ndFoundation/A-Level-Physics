# Gravitational-Field-Line Diagram

## Core Idea

A gravitational-field-line diagram represents a gravitational field by drawing lines whose direction gives the direction of the gravitational force on a mass and whose density represents the [[Gravitational-Field-Strength]].

## Form

Lines always point *toward* mass (gravity is only attractive — there is no repulsive counterpart). Around a spherical body like a planet the lines are radial, pointing inward. Close to a planet's surface, over a small region, the lines are very nearly uniform: parallel, equally spaced and vertically downward, which is why `g ≈ 9.81 N kg⁻¹` is treated as constant in projectile problems.

## Axes / Labels / Components

Not a graph. Components:

- Field lines with arrows pointing toward the mass.
- The source mass (planet, star), drawn to scale where helpful.
- Line spacing: closer lines = stronger field (e.g. denser near the surface).

## Physical Meaning

The tangent to a line gives the direction of the gravitational force on a test mass; the density of lines gives relative field strength. The radial pattern far from a planet corresponds to the inverse-square law `g = GM/r²`; the locally uniform pattern near the surface corresponds to the approximately constant `g` used in mechanics.

## Gradient / Area / Intercepts

Not applicable to the diagram. The associated relationship is `g = F/m` (force per unit mass), with `g = GM/r²` for a radial field.

## Converts To / From

- From: a mass configuration (planet, two-body system).
- To: [[Gravitational-Field-Strength]] estimates from line density and the radial/uniform approximation.

## Related Quantities

- [[Gravitational-Field-Strength]]

## Related Methods

- Comparing line density to compare field strength
- Treating the field as uniform near a surface

## Common Mistakes

- Drawing field lines pointing away from a mass (gravity is attractive only).
- Treating the near-surface field as radial when the uniform approximation is intended (and vice versa).
- Letting lines cross.
