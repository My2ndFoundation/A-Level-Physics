# Free-Body Diagram

## Core Idea

A free-body diagram isolates a single object and represents every external [[Force]] acting *on it* as a labelled arrow. It is the essential first step before applying [[Newton-Second-Law]] or resolving forces.

## Form

The object is reduced to a point or a simple box. Each force is drawn as an arrow starting from that object: the arrow's direction is the direction of the force and its length is roughly proportional to the force's magnitude. Only forces acting *on* the chosen object are shown — forces the object exerts on other things are excluded.

## Axes / Labels / Components

- The object: a dot or small box (the "free body").
- Weight: $W = mg$ arrow pointing vertically down from the centre.
- Normal contact force: arrow perpendicular to and away from the surface.
- Tension / thrust: along strings, rods or springs.
- Friction or drag: along the surface or fluid, opposing relative motion.
- Applied/driving force: as specified.
- Every arrow is labelled with a symbol and, where known, a value and unit.

## Physical Meaning

The diagram converts a physical scenario into the set of vectors that determine motion. If the arrows balance (vector sum zero) the object is in equilibrium; if they do not, the resultant equals `ma` by [[Newton-Second-Law]].

## Gradient / Area / Intercepts

Not applicable — this is a diagram, not a graph. The analytic step is **resolving** each arrow into perpendicular components and summing them to find the resultant force.

## Converts To / From

- From: a real situation or a sketch showing contacts and fields.
- To: resolved component equations and then a [[Newton-Second-Law]] calculation.

## Related Quantities

- [[Force]]
- [[Acceleration]]

## Related Methods

- Resolving forces into components
- [[Using-Gradient]] (when forces feed into a graph-based analysis)

## Common Mistakes

- Drawing forces the object exerts on others (e.g. the push back on a hand) instead of forces on the object.
- Inventing a "force of motion" in the direction of travel — motion does not need a forward force.
- Omitting weight or the normal contact force.
- Drawing the Newton's third law pair on the same diagram (the pair acts on different bodies).
