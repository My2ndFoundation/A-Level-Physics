# Hertzsprung Russell Diagram

## Core Idea

The Hertzsprung–Russell (H–R) diagram is a scatter plot of stars by
[[Luminosity]] against surface temperature. It reveals that stars fall into
distinct groups and traces the path of [[Stellar-Evolution]].

## Form

A two-dimensional scatter diagram. Each point is one star. Three regions
dominate: the diagonal **main sequence**, the **giants/supergiants** region
(upper right) and the **white dwarfs** region (lower left).

## Axes / Labels / Components

- **x-axis:** surface temperature, plotted *decreasing left to right* (hot
  blue stars on the left, cool red on the right) — often logarithmic. Spectral
  classes O B A F G K M follow this order.
- **y-axis:** luminosity (W) or luminosity relative to the Sun, usually
  logarithmic, increasing upwards.
- **Components:** main sequence (a diagonal band from hot-bright to
  cool-faint), red giants and supergiants (cool but very luminous),
  white dwarfs (hot but faint).

## Physical Meaning

A star's position is set by its temperature and power output. Most stars lie
on the main sequence, fusing hydrogen with gravity balanced by radiation
pressure. As a star evolves it moves off the main sequence into the giant
region and finally to the white-dwarf region (for low/medium mass stars).
Position therefore encodes both the star's current state and its mass.

## Gradient / Area / Intercepts

Not a quantitative line graph, so gradient and area have no direct meaning.
However, lines of constant radius run diagonally because $L = 4\pi r^2\sigma T^4$
([[Stefans-Law]]): for fixed luminosity, cooler stars must be larger, which
is why giants sit above and right of the main sequence.

## Converts To / From

- From: stellar spectra (temperature via [[Wiens-Displacement-Law]]) and
  measured brightness plus distance (luminosity)
- To: classification of stars and their evolutionary stage

## Common Mistakes

- Plotting temperature increasing left to right (it decreases)
- Reading the main sequence as a time-ordered track for one star
- Forgetting the axes are usually logarithmic

## Visuals

### H-R diagram: schematic with main sequence, giants, and white dwarfs

![[_attachments/08_Representations/hertzsprung-russell-diagram--schematic.svg]]
*Figure: [[Luminosity]] (log scale, y-axis increasing upward) plotted against surface temperature (log scale, x-axis DECREASING leftward — hot blue-white O-stars at left, cool red M-stars at right). The main sequence (blue band) runs diagonally from hot-bright (upper left) to cool-faint (lower right). Red giants and supergiants (orange/yellow region) sit upper-right — cool but very luminous, so very large radii (from [[Stefans-Law]]: $L = 4\pi r^2\sigma T^4$). White dwarfs (light-blue region) sit lower-left — hot but faint, so very small. The Sun (☉) lies on the main sequence at ~5 800 K and $L = L_\odot$.*
