# Circuit Diagram

## Core Idea

A circuit diagram is a standardised symbolic drawing of an electrical circuit. It strips away the physical layout and shows only how components are electrically connected, so the circuit can be analysed and reproduced unambiguously.

## Form

Components are drawn as standard British Standards symbols connected by straight lines representing ideal (zero-resistance) connecting wires. Wires meeting at a junction are shown with a dot; wires crossing without connecting have no dot. Series and parallel arrangements are made explicit by the topology, not by physical position.

## Axes / Labels / Components

Not a graph — the "components" are the circuit symbols:

- Cell / battery, switch, lamp, fixed and variable resistor.
- Ammeter (placed in series to measure [[Current]]).
- Voltmeter (placed in parallel to measure [[Potential-Difference]]).
- LDR, thermistor, diode, LED, capacitor, potentiometer.
- Junctions marked with dots; each component usually labelled with a symbol and value.

## Physical Meaning

The diagram defines the electrical relationships needed to apply Kirchhoff's laws and [[Ohms-Law]]: which components share the same current (series), which share the same potential difference (parallel), and where a cell's [[Internal-Resistance]] sits.

## Gradient / Area / Intercepts

Not applicable. The analytic operations are identifying series/parallel groupings, applying conservation of charge at junctions, and conservation of energy around loops.

## Converts To / From

- From: a real breadboard or apparatus arrangement.
- To: circuit equations, and graphs such as the [[IV-Characteristic]] obtained from a measuring circuit.

## Related Quantities

- [[Current]]
- [[Potential-Difference]]
- [[Resistance]]

## Related Methods

- Applying Kirchhoff's laws
- [[Using-Gradient]] (when circuit data is graphed)

## Common Mistakes

- Placing an ammeter in parallel or a voltmeter in series.
- Drawing junction dots ambiguously so series and parallel cannot be told apart.
- Treating connecting wires as having resistance when the standard model assumes ideal wires.
