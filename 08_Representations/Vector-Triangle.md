# Vector Triangle

## Core Idea

A vector triangle is a scale diagram in which two vectors are drawn tip-to-tail and the third side represents their resultant, giving a geometric way to add or balance vectors.

## Form

Each vector is drawn as an arrow whose length is proportional to its magnitude and whose orientation is its direction. The second vector starts where the first ends ("tip-to-tail"). The closing side, drawn from the start of the first arrow to the tip of the last, is the resultant. When three vectors are in equilibrium they form a *closed* triangle (the triangle of forces), because their resultant is zero.

## Axes / Labels / Components

There are no fixed axes — it is a scaled spatial diagram. Label each side with its quantity and value, mark the chosen scale (e.g. 1 cm = 10 N), and mark angles measured with a protractor. Arrowheads must show direction and follow tip-to-tail (resultant arrow points the opposite way round, from overall start to overall end).

## Physical Meaning

The triangle expresses that vector quantities combine by their directions, not by simple addition ([[Vectors-and-Scalars]]). A closed triangle means a system is in equilibrium: e.g. weight, tension and a horizontal force on a hanging object balance, so the [[Resultant-Force]] is zero.

## Gradient / Area / Intercepts

Not applicable — this is a geometric construction, not a plotted graph. The information is in the *lengths* (magnitudes) and *angles* (directions) of the sides, read off by scale and protractor or computed using the sine and cosine rules.

## Converts To / From

- From: a set of vector quantities (forces, velocities, displacements)
- To: a single resultant vector, or a confirmation of equilibrium; equivalently obtainable by [[Resolving-Vectors]]

## Common Mistakes

- Drawing vectors head-to-head instead of tip-to-tail
- Forgetting a consistent scale, so lengths are not proportional
- Reversing the resultant arrow's direction
- Using a triangle when components ([[Resolving-Vectors]]) would be cleaner, or vice versa
